Observation W verse Additional notes
Paul who Rom 1:1 the rarely disputed author of letter, coverted on the Damascus Road from Saul the persecutor to Paul the apostle
- a servant of Christ what Rom 1:1 some estimate the poplulation of Rome at the time to 60% - 80% slaves, Paul immediately garners acceptance from
his Gentile audience by lighting himself as a "slave" to Christ
- called to be an apostle what Rom 1:1 Paul next informs his audience that he was "called" to apostleship by God, not appointed by man
- set apart for the Gospel what Rom 1:1 in addition to be "called", Paul is also "set apart" for the Gospel of God. This imagery would have garnered immediate
acceptance from his Jewish audience, as the OT Jews understand the concept of being "set apart"
The Gospel
- the Gospel He promised what Rom 1:2 the Gospel is not a new message of faith, but one fastened firminly on the outstanding promises of God in the OT
- beforehand through prophetswhat Rom 1:2 the message of Christ brought by Paul is not a new faith, but the continuation of the same message brought in the OT
the word "prophets" does not only refer to the books at the end of the OT, but is a way of referring to it as a whole
- in the Holy Scriptures what Rom 1:2 Paul's writing does not cancel the validity or sanctity of the Hebrew Bible, he writes from their authority
Regarding His Son
- to his earhtly life what Rom 1:3 Paul's makes a tacit confession that Jesus has a life that exists outside of this earthly one. See John 1:1
- was a descendant of David what Rom 1:3 in accordance with OT prophecy, the Messiah must a descendant of David. The "forever king" to come from his line
- through the Spirit what Rom 1:4 the Spirit's work in the life of Christ is part of the Lord's Messianic Identity
- apointed the Son of God what Rom 1:4 the word used here for "apointed" or "declared" is the same root from which we get our English word "horizon"
this verse is not suggesting the Holy Spirit made Jesus the Son of God, He is the eternal Son of God, but through the working
of the Spirit, a clear distinction is made between Jesus and the rest of Humanity through His resurrection as evidence
- by His resurrection what Rom 1:4 the resurrection was and still is one of the greatest apologetics for the Christian Faith, clearly it was a widely known fact
- Jesus Christ our Lord who Rom 1:4 the word Paul uses here for "Lord" is /kur-i-os/ and it denotes one who is under the command or authority of another
Through Christ
- we have received grace what Rom 1:5 Paul now begins to "open up" the Gospel by illustrating that grace is and always as been work of God, not man
- and apostleship what Rom 1:5 Paul calling to ministry was just to "serve God" wherever he saw fit, but his calling to minister in the name of Christ as well
- to call all gentiles who Rom 1:5 Paul's identity as the "apostle to the Gentiles" does not stop him from seeing the original apostles, but strengthens it
- to obedience what Rom 1:5 the goal of Paul's gospel preaching is not merely education, but to bring the Gentiles to obedient living before God
- that comes through faith what Rom 1:5 obedience is the "fruit' of a life of faith. Paul intends to minister to bring Gentiles into obedient living
- for His names sake what Rom 1:5 the one who ultimately receives the praise and glory for our obedience is not us, but God. It is HE who is praised because
the only way we are able to live obeidently is because of His grace.
You (recipients)
- are among those Gentiles who Rom 1:6 Paul clearly identifies a part of his intended audience, the Gentile Christians of Rome
- called to belong to Christ who Rom 1:6 Paul uses the same terminology of his calling to ministry as he does to the Gentile Roman Christian's calling. There is debate
as to whether this calling is for salvation or servanthood. But, regardless of the choice, Paul sees them as "called"
- To all in Rome who Rom 1:7 the letter is clearly addressed to the Christian community as a whole in the city of Rome, this includes Jews & Gentiles
- who are loved by God who Rom 1:7 the Christians Paul is writing to, he has never seen or met, are surely loved by God,
called to be his holy people who Rom 1:7 Paul, as a Pharisee would have understood the clear OT overtones of being "called" to be a holy people. This understanding
is now being applied to the Gentile and Jews who are "in Rome" and "loved by God".
- grace and peace to you what Rom 1:7 a common Pauline greeting used by the Apostle (Rom 1:7, 1 Cor 1:3, 2 Cor 1:2, Gal 1:3, Eph 1:2, Phi 1:2, Col 1:2, 1 Thes 1:1,
2 Thes 1:2, Tit 1:4, Phlm 1:3)
- God our Father who Rom 1:7 Paul sees all of his recipients as fellow "children" of God the Father, both Jews and Gentiles
- the Lord Jesus Christ who Rom 1:7 Paul also sees all of his recipients as fellow "citizens" of the Savior's Kingdom, both Jews and Gentiles
