  Genesis: An Introduction

Author: Traditional and Alternate View

Jewish and Christian traditions hold the author of the Torah or Pentateuch (first five books of the Bible) to be Moses. The Pentateuch claims Moses authorship for itself (Exodus 17:14, 24:27, 34:27, Numbers 33:1-2, Deuteronomy 31:9). Other books of the Old Testament claim this also (Joshua 1:7-8, 1 Kings 2:3, Ezra 6:18, 2 Chronicles 25:4, Daniel 9:11-13, and Malachi 4:4). Mosaic authorship is also the view of the New Testament also (Mark 12:26, Luke 24:27, John 5:46, and 2 Corinthians 3:15). 

An alternate view hold that Moses was not the author of the Torah because it was not written by a single author, but it’s a compilation of several authors works, this theory is called, “Documentary Hypothesis”. This view began in the post-enlightenment period and is based largely if not entirely on the assumption that the Bible is a purely human product, not divine Scripture. This theory is also known as the “JEDP” theory as it suggests there were four sources (Jahwistic, Elohistic, Deuteronomic, and Priestly) comprising what we now know as the Pentateuch. This idea is often supported by the fact that there are different names used for God in the text of the Pentateuch. This mistake of this view is to see the different names as an effect of different sources rather than the differing emphasis the text is making. This was the dominant view of Biblical scholarship for some time until recent years when conservative scholars have defended the traditional view. 

Date: completed around 1406 BC

We must remember that the date of composition and the time of the narrative events told in the composition are not the same thing. The narrative time at the beginning of the Genesis is eternity past. There is no way to know how long God existed in and of His own nature before deciding to create, “the heavens and the earth” in Genesis 1:1. The narrative time begins at creation. However, Moses did not live until after all of the events of Genesis had passed. Moses likely wrote the Torah during the 40 year wilderness wandering after the Exodus. The date for the Pentateuch is calculated from the time Solomon began construction of the temple mentioned in 1 Kings 6:1. 

Construction began in the 4th year of Solomon’s reign……………………… 966 BC

This happened 480 years after the Exodus from Egypt……………………… 1446 BC

The wilderness wandering lasted 40 years after Exodus…………………… 1406 BC

The writing of the Torah must have occurred before……………………….. 1406 BC

Purpose: several key purposes

Genesis is the second longest book in the Bible by volume. To ascertain the purpose of the document, we must examine the text of Genesis alone and as part of the whole of God’s revealed Word. When asked, Jesus stated the whole purpose of the Scriptures is to love God, so first and foremost the purpose of all Scripture is to draw us to a loving relationship with our Creator. Genesis also creates the theological foundations of sin, depravity, need for redemption and the expectation of the Messiah. There are also several types or foreshadows of Christ found in Genesis further pointing to the coming King and Kings. 

Outline: Primeval History and Patriarchal History

The outline and structure of Genesis is divided into two sections: Primeval History and Patriarchal History. Each of these sections are then further divided into five narrative sections. The 10 clearly marked sections of Genesis are referred to as, “Toledoth” sections. Toledoth is a Hebrew word meaning to carry or bear, but is often translated as, “genealogy, generations, lineage, descendants, account, etc.”. These 10 Toledoth sections comprise the outline of the book of Genesis. 

Primeval History

The written account of the heavens and the earth (1:1 – 4:26)

The written account of Adam (5:1 – 6:8) 

The written account of Noah (6:9 – 9:29)

The written account of Noah’s sons (10:1 – 11:9) 

The written account of Shem (11:10-26)


Patriarchal History 


The written account of Terah (11:27 – 25:11)

The written account of Ishmael (25:12-18)

The written account of Isaac (25:19 – 35:29)

The written account of Esau (36:1 – 43) 

The written account of Jacob (37:1 – 50:26)

With this outline, it is easy to see that while the topics of both primeval and patriarchal history are both given, the weight of the book is focused more on the patriarchal history of Israel and the established promises and expectations to come from them. This foundation allows Genesis to not only usher in the Scriptures, but set the foundation and guiding understanding of the rest of God’s Word. God’s love for a fallen humanity drives a covenant relationship which expects and awaits the return of the now resurrected and glorified Savior King, Jesus.
