There was a  | what | Luke 16:19 | the first question we must face is whether we believe this is a parable or a factual description of the afterlife. Several 
 |  |  | details point to it being more than a parable: (1) named characters (2) not based on an observable detail ie, sheep/coins
 |  |  | (3) it portrays the afterlife in an unexpected way ie, no banquet.    However, Jesus is using the story to teach a greater 
 |  |  | point which makes it parabolic in that sense.  Regardless of which way you see it, the Savior's ulimate points are not
 |  |  | changed, but the details will be directly affected by your view of whether this is parabolic or factual.

purple and fine linen | what | Luke 16:19 | this is a deeper description of the oppulance in which this man lives. Purple was the most expensive cloth of the day
 |  |  | the fine linen can refer to his outer or inner garments and thus ilustrate the 'best of the best' clothing
lived in luxury | what | Luke 16:19 | a description of being served the finest foods and living in relaxation, Luke is deepening the "richness" of this man's life
at his gate | where | Luke 16:20 | the fact that the rich man's estate had a gate is an additional illustration of his wealth and stature 
beggar named Lazarus | who | Luke 16:20 | this is not the same Lazarus as in John 11, this man is poor with no family, unlike the rich man, this Lazarus must beg
