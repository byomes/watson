Introduction to the book of Joshua

Author: 

The author is never stated in the Biblical text, though tradition holds that Joshua himself wrote most of it himself. This hypothesis is supported by the multiple firsthand accounts of details that a present observer would have seen and the mentioning of Rahab still being alive when it is written (6:25). Conversely, Joshua’s death is recorded in the book (24:29-33) so obviously he did not write this or anything following. The fact that an appendix was added to the book, possibly by Eleazar or an elder who outlived Joshua, does not undermine his own authoring of most of the book. 

Date: 

The Exodus and subsequent conquest of Egypt can be calculated off the building of Solomon’s Temple. In 1 Kings 6:1 we read Solomon began building the Temple 480 years after the Exodus from Egypt. This took place is 966 BC. This places the date for the Exodus 480 years earlier in 1446 BC. Now adding back in 40 years for the wilderness wandering period, the events of the book of Joshua likely begin in 1405 or 1406 BC. The book spans approximately 30 years covering events from 1405 to 1375 BC. 

Purpose: 

The purpose of the book of Joshua is to illustrate the fulfillment of the promises God made to the patriarchs in Genesis that the chosen people of God would inherit the land. The story of the Exodus cannot be separated from the Conquest of Canaan. They are two halves of the same promise fulfillment from God to His people. Theologically, the book of Joshua illustrates two main lessons: (a) God is faithful and (b) blessing comes through obedience. 

Outline and Theme: 

The book of Joshua can be outlined to four main points. In my (Pastor Bill) opinion, the central theme of the book is the inheritance, not conquering, of the land. This highlights God’s divine action in giving the land to His people rather than the taking of the land by military force. Israel is not absolved their responsibility to “walk by faith” which will require engaging in warfare, but the ultimate victory and subsequent inheritance is provided by the divine will God, not by military might of man.

Receiving the inheritance. (6:1-12:24)

Distributing the inheritance. (13:1-21:45)
