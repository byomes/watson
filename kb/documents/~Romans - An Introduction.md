Romans: An Introduction

Author: 

	Pauline authorship of the Book of Romans is virtually guaranteed by the scholarly community. While some of the other New Testament letters may garner debate when it comes to their authorship, Romans does not. The letter to the Romans was written by the first century pharisee turned apostle, Paul (Saul) of Tarsus. Paul was a man born a Jew, who lived in the Greco-Roman world of learning, and died a Christian apostle and missionary. Saul was born in Tarsus on the Roman isle of Cilicia (Acts 22:3). He was from the tribe of Benjamin and followed in the strictest sect of Judaism in his day as a Pharisee (Philippians 3:5).  He was a student of the well-respected Jewish rabbi Gamaliel (Acts 22:3). Paul was a very learned man and is called the “apostle to the Gentiles” (Romans 11:13). It is good to remember that Paul had not planted nor visited the Christian community in Rome prior to writing his letter. 

Date: 

	Paul wrote the letter to the Romans from Corinth on his third missionary journey. Paul mentions his plans to travel to Jerusalem, Rome, and then Spain in Romans 15. He also mentions several individuals specifically Phoebe from Cencrea which is a town about seven miles southeast of Corinth. The best estimate for the writing of Romans is between AD55 – AD57. 

Purpose: 

	There is debate when it comes to answering the question, “Why did Paul write the letter to the Romans?” Three theories have been advanced to attempt to define the purpose for this book: a theological purpose, a pastoral purpose, and/or a missionary purpose. There is no need to see these options as “the only” view, but rather a good student should evaluate how their preconceptions of the letter affect their reading of it. 

In the theological view, Romans is Paul’s theological treatise teaching the Romans about the deep topics of sin, justification, faith, sanctification and the like. The position here is to see the book of Romans as a summary of Paul’s theology. This could be as a defense of his upcoming visit to Jerusalem or as a means of raising funds for his journey to Spain. 

In the pastoral view, first century history plays a larger role. The edict of Claudius (AD49) expelled all the Jews from the capitol city until his death in AD54. This caused the Roman church to be solely gentile for nearly five years. After which time Jewish Christians returned to the city and the Christian community. The pastoral view views the letter to the Romans as Paul’s attempt to help bring unity to the conflict between the Jewish and Gentile Christians attempting to reintegrate their communities. 

The missionary view finds much of its context in Paul’s missionary journeys and the gentile offering collected for the Jerusalem church (Romans 15:25-29). During his missionary journeys, a famine hit the Jerusalem area hard and Paul collected money from the churches around the Mediterranean to bring them support. Likewise, as this view suggests, Paul writes “ahead” to the Roman Church extoling the unity of the Church and entreating them to support his next journey to take the Gospel to Spain (the end of the earth in Paul’s day) (Acts 1:8). 

It is my (Pastor Bill) opinion that each view finds support in the letter and thus a combined purpose may be tenable. The pastoral view holds the most support, as it gives a strong foundation for more of the book than the other two. However, the theological purpose can be seen as uniting the Jewish and Gentile communities in hopes of mobilizing them to fund his upcoming journey to Spain thereby incorporating the missionary purpose as well.
