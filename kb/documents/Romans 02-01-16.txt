Observation W verse Additional notes
You who Rom 2:1 the Jewish & Gentile Christians in Rome after the death of Claudius in 54AD when the Jews returned to the Church community
- therefore, have no excuse what Rom 2:1 the "therefore" is a continuation of the argument from the end of the previous chapter, the wrath of God is evident because
of the sinfulness of people who not only "do such things" but also "approve of those" who do. Because of this…
- who pass judgment what Rom 2:1 the classic man's response to our sin is to find "quality by comparison" and consider ourselves greater than another person
- condem yourself what Rom 2:1 when we engage in judging someone else's sin, we are also passing judgment on ourselves because we are also sinners
- whatever point you judge what Rom 2:1 the "judgment" we have on others is not an eternal judgment, because we do not have the authority to do this. Just as at the
end of chapter one sinners were "approving" of others sin, so too the righteous (or those that think they are) are "judging"
the sinfulness of others not realizing the very act of judging someone else judges yourself as well
God's judgment what Rom 2:2 Paul makes a distinction between the judgment of God (based on truth), and human judgment (based on our own opinions)
- based on truth what Rom 2:2 God's judgment is always true because He is truth (John 14:6) He is the unchanging standard to which we are measured
we, mere human being who Rom 2:3 Paul makes another distinction between the sovereign royalty of God and the "mere" humanity of man
- pass judgment what Rom 2:3 passing judgment in this sense is the condemnation of that which is wrong, we do this to one another all the time
- do the same things what Rom 2:3 in our human hypocrisy we both condem someone else's actions and then turn around and commit our own
- will escape judgment? what Rom 2:3 the question here is obviously one of rhetorial sarcasm, of course we will not escape judgment, but when we engage in the
act of judging others, we are placing ourselves in the authoritative position of one who is able (or has the right to) judge
- show contempt for what Rom 2:4 when we judge others we not only condem ourselves, but we also show contempt (to think little or less of) God's patience
- God's kindness… patience what Rom 2:4 we declare that we no longer care (or care less than we did before) of the love and patience of God when we judge others.
we reject the truth that God has shown us more patience than we deserve when we refuse to have patience with others
- lead to repentance what Rom 2:4 the kindness and patience of God is intended to lead us to repentance, His love should draw us to Himself.
- stubborn & unrepentant heartwhat Rom 2:5 Paul comes back to describing the "you" in the first verse, not illustrating the stubborn and unrepentant heart of sinful man
- storing up wrath what Rom 2:5 the outcome of our "stubborn and unrepentant" heart is the "storing up" of wrath. We give God more reasons to judge us
when we engage in judging others
- day of God's wrath what Rom 2:5 this is heavy with Old Testament understanding of the "great and terrible Day of the Lord" when He brings judgment on mankind
- judgment revealed what Rom 2:5 the day of judgment is coming, God's judgment is the only judgment that is "righteous" because He is the without sin judge
- God will repay what Rom 2:6 the repayment in this verse is the repayment of a debt or the payment of a wage, in both instances the "earning" has already
been done and this is the action of fulfilling the payment due for the action(s) previously taken.
- what they have done what Rom 2:6 the actions we engage in (like judging others) are the "wages" for which repayment is required is earned in our actions
- Eternal life what Rom 2:7 the "good" wage of life spend with the Father in an eternal state
- persist doing goodwhat Rom 2:7 this is not a "faith by works" scenario, but rather the judment that saving faith will have evidence in the outworkings of action
- wrath and anger what Rom 2:8 just like the OT "day of the Lord" the righteous judge of Israel will pour out his wrath on the sinful and faithless
- reject the truth what Rom 2:8 the "day of the Lord" will be a happy and sad day, those who reject God's truth will receive the wages of their own sin
Every human being what Rom 2:9 Paul reiterates the "everyone" nature of his teaching, the Day of the Lord is coming and everyone will be made to account
- trouble and distress what Rom 2:9 the "day of the Lord" will be a time when the alluring beauty of sin is exposed for the death rot that it really is
- who does evil what Rom 2:9 just as faith is an action of the heart worked out in the body, so too evil starts in the heart and works its way out in the body
- first Jew, then Gentile what Rom 2:9 Paul again confirms Irsael's special place in God's redemptive plan by being the vehicle through which salvation comes
he also directly addresses again the dual nature of his audience, Jew & Gentile Christians in Rome
- glory, honor, and peace what Rom 2:10 there will be glory, honor, and peace, the polar opposites of "trouble and distress" in the Day of the Lord
- who does good what Rom 2:10 again, Paul is not espousing a works-based faith, but merely a heart of faith will reveal itself in the actions of the faithful
- first Jew, then Gentile who Rom 2:10 notice that Paul mentions the Jew/Gentile dichotomoy on both the condemened and redeemed sides of judgment day
God does not show favoritism what Rom 2:11 God will not make distinctions on the Day of Judgment based on ethnicity or theology (Jew/Gentile), but on the basis of faith
sin apart from the Law who Rom 2:12 this is a reference to Gentile sin, gentiles sin "outside" the Law because they are not bound to live by the Law
sin under the Law who Rom 2:12 this is a reference to Jewish sin, Jews sin "under" the Law because the Law was given to them
not those who hear the Law who Rom 2:13 Paul points out that merely "hearing" the Law (being a Jew) does not equate to salvation, obedience by faith is required
Gentiles… law for themselves who Rom 2:14 even though the Gentile were never given the Law (God's standard for how we as His chosen people should live) when they
live by faith, they are fulfilling the same purpose as the Law. Namely, to please God by obedience through faith (Rom 1:5)
written on their hearts what Rom 2:15 Paul is referencing an Old Testament prophecy from Jeremiah 31:33 "I will put my law within them, and I will write it on their
hearts." The Gentile addition to the Church of Jesus Christ was not an afterthought but a fulfillment of OT prophecy
- their conscience / thoughts what Rom 2:15 the effect of having the Law "written on the heart" is the mind is then bent towards things of God and away from things of sin
will take place what Rom 2:16 The future expectation of Judgement Day or the Day of the Lord is still a future expectation
- God judges people's secrets what Rom 2:16 There is no part of our human experience that will be able to be hidden from God on the day of judgment
- through Christ what Rom 2:16 God will judge all humans THROUGH Christ, faith in Christ will be the sole lens by which all of humanity will be judged
