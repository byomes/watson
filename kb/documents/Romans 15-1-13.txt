Observation W verse Additional notes
We who are strong who Rom 15:1 with no obvious transition, this "strong" should be understood as the "stronger" believer from the previous chapter. Ie, the one
who does not abstain from meat or hold certain days to be holier than others.
ought to bear what Rom 15:1 the word used here carries the connotation of carrying with the hands or carrying with one's own strength
the failings of the weak what Rom 15:1 in the opening verses of the previous chapter, Paul called the stronger believers (likely Gentiles) to accept and not treat with
contempt the weaker (likely Jewish) believers who were still holding to the food laws of the Old Testament. Here, Paul now
gives the same group a charge to carry the weight of their frustration with them
not to please ourselves what Rom 15:1 given the context, Paul is urging those who have their freedom in Christ and are not bound by the desires to follow the Law
to bear with patience those who do, not to force the weaker brother to the stronger way of thinking to make it easier
Paul urges the "stronger" believers to willing give up some of their Chrisitan liberty to better serve the weaker brothers
please our neighbors who Rom 15:2 in a gospel context, one's neightbor is whoever they are with, not just physical or theological neighbors
for their good what Rom 15:2 the motivating factor in how a stronger believer treats a weaker one is the benefit of the weaker, not the stronger
to build them up what Rom 15:2 the goal of treating the weaker brother's good is to build them up to be stronger, not cater to their weakness
for even Christ who Rom 15:3 Paul uses Christ as the ultimate example of someone who did what was best for the "weaker" rather than Himself
fallen on me what Rom 15:3 the sin of the world was laid upon Christ when He was sinless, this is our ultimate example of humble sacrifice
everything that was written what Rom 15:4 Paul refers here to the written Scritpures, the Hebrew Bible, or our Old Testament
written to teach us what Rom 15:4 the purpose of the Holy Scriptrues is not to comfort or coddle us, but rather to teach us
endurance / encouragement what Rom 15:4 the things we are supposed to receive from the Scriptures are endurance and encouragment (to remain faithful)
have hope what Rom 15:4 the end goal of our increased endurance and encouragement is the hope we share in Christ. This is a unifying factor as
both Jewish and Gentile Christians share the same need for endurance and encouragement and share the same future in Christ
may the God who gives what Rom 15:5 though we gain endurance and encouragment from reading the Scriptures, it is ultimatley God who grants us those gifts
same attitude as Christ what Rom 15:5 Christians are never justified in treating one another any differently than Christ would. We are His subjects and must reflect Him
with one mind & voice what Rom 15:6 the unity of the Church is the proper outcome of Christ-like character and treatment of fellow believers.
Accept one another then what Rom 15:7 this comes as a command, based on the previous verses to accept one another, this further illustrates the pastoral view
and underscores the division between the Jewish and Gentile believers in Rome at the time
as Christ accepted you what Rom 15:7 as Christ has accepted all of us, despite our failings and sin, we are thus required to accept one another
to bring praise to God what Rom 15:7 the unity and acceptance of the Church to itself brings praise to God
Christ became a servant what Rom 15:8 Christ is viewed again as the example of self-sacrifice and humility, choosing to be a servant for mankind
of the Jews who Rom 15:8 as Messiah, Christ serves the Jewish people
so that promises made what Rom 15:8 the outstanding promises of God are what drove Jesus to fulfill His role as Messiah and accept all who believe in Him
the patriarchs who Rom 15:8 Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, the father's the Jewish people
Gentiles might glorify God who Rom 15:9 the long term effects of Abraham's promises being fulfilled is the praise the Gentile would bring to God Almighty. The context
here suggests this praise is for the salvation brought to the Gentile people of the world.
Old Testament Quote what Rom 15:9b-12the old testament quotes given by Paul here illustrate that it was always God's plan for the Gentiles to come to know Him
fill you with joy and peace what Rom 15:13 the internal and personal benefit of being filled with joy and peace is the outcome of trusting in God
overflow with hope what Rom 15:13 when we are filled with joy and peace, we will overflow with hope for the future and what Christ can/will do
