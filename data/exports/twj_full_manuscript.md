# **INTRODUCTION**

## **The Most Dangerous Kind of Wrong**

I was standing in the middle of a church in Tanzania when it hit me.

I didn't understand a word being sung. The language was Swahili, the instruments were unfamiliar, and I was thousands of miles from anything I would normally call home. But something stopped me. Because what I was hearing, whatever it was called in that language, in that place, sounded exactly like worship.

Not similar to worship. Not reminiscent of it. It sounded exactly like worship. The real thing.

I've been in enough churches over the years to know the difference. There is a sound the people of God make when they mean it. It crosses every language, every cultural boundary, every stylistic preference. And standing in that sanctuary in Tanzania, I heard it unmistakably. Kingdom Worship sounds like Kingdom Worship, even when you don't understand a single word being sung.

That moment gave me something I've been sitting with ever since, and it's the reason this book exists.

Because here is the uncomfortable question that experience put in my head: if genuine worship sounds like that, that real, that unmistakable, that full, then how do we know it's aimed at the right Jesus?

The building was a single room, mud-brick walls and a metal roof, plastic chairs lined up in rows on a dirt floor. I was worshiping with the Maasai people, a herding people who still hunt lions with spears. On the drive to the church that morning, we had passed giraffes eating from trees the way we see deer grazing along roadsides back home in the eastern United States. A boy who couldn't have been more than seven or eight, dressed in traditional Maasai clothing, was guiding a flock of small goats alone down the side of the road. A little further on, a larger flock of cattle and goats was being watched by a team of young men, and moving among their animals, completely unbothered, were wild zebras.

After the service, we ate together: fresh goat slaughtered that morning and rice. It was hands down the most memorable meal of the whole trip.

I had asked my new friends why lions don't attack the Maasai's animals. They laughed at me. Clearly, I asked a silly question. Then one of the men explained: I could walk through lion country wearing a traditional Maasai shuka, the red cloth wrap they gave me, and the lions would leave me alone. But if I walked into that same territory in my regular "Western" clothes, I would be in danger because the lions know the difference. They recognize the Maasai. They know who belongs in that landscape and who doesn't.

I've thought about that a lot since.

Because standing in that mud-brick church, listening to worship in a language I couldn't understand, I heard something I recognized completely. The People of God sound like the Church of God, even in Swahili, even under a metal roof, even surrounded by a people who walk through lion country without fear.

We have built a lot of assumptions about what makes worship valid. We measure it by intensity: how moved we are, how loudly we sing, how many hands go up. We measure it by style: the right music, the right building, the right tradition. We measure it by sincerity: if I really mean it, it must be right.

But none of those things, on their own, tell you anything about whether your worship is accurate.

That is a distinction most of us have never been asked to make. We have been asked whether our worship is genuine. We have been asked whether our faith is sincere. We have rarely, if ever, been asked whether the Jesus we are worshiping is actually Jesus.

Palm Sunday is the moment in Scripture that forces us to face this question, and it is more uncomfortable than it first appears.

On the day Jesus rode into Jerusalem, which is commonly referred to as Palm Sunday, there were people in the crowd gathered at the roadside who were doing everything right by any external measure. They were present. They were passionate. They were quoting Scripture. They were laying down their cloaks, cutting branches, and shouting praises to the Son of David. If you had filmed that crowd and shown it to any church growth consultant in the country, they would have called it a revival.

And many of them were worshiping the wrong Jesus.

Not a different person. The actual, incarnate, in-the-flesh Son of God was right there in front of them, and they were getting Him wrong. They were projecting onto Him everything they wanted Him to be, everything they needed Him to do, everything their history had taught them to expect. They weren't hypocrites. They weren't cynical. They weren't faking it. They were wholeheartedly, passionately, sincerely wrong.

That is the most dangerous kind of wrong there is. Because it feels exactly like being right.

If that crowd, people who had the physical Jesus in front of them, who had access to many of the same Scriptures we have, who were worshiping with everything they had, if they could get Jesus that wrong while worshiping Him that genuinely, then there is no reason to assume you and I are exempt.

In fact, there is every reason to suspect that we can and do make the same mistake today. Not out of malice or laziness, but out of the deeply human tendency to shape our Savior around what we need Him to be, rather than receiving Him as He actually is.

Palm Sunday is a warning and not a comfortable one.

***You can passionately worship the wrong Jesus.***

This book is an attempt to figure out what that means, and what to do about it.

## **A Note Before We Begin**

The question this book is built around is not designed to be answered once and set aside. It is designed to become a permanent part of how you follow the Savior. By the time you finish, I hope it is living somewhere in the back of your mind, surfacing in the moments that matter most:

> *Am I following Jesus, or am I following what I want Him to be?*

That question is not an accusation. It is an invitation. The crowd on Palm Sunday did not have the resurrection, the completed New Testament Scriptures, or the indwelling Holy Spirit that you and I have access to today. We are without excuse in ways they were not. But that also means we have resources they didn't have, resources sufficient to find our way back to the real Jesus every time we drift.

That's what this book is for.

Let's begin.

# **PART ONE: THE PROBLEM**

# **CHAPTER ONE**

## **The Parade We All Join**

Nobody comes to Jesus as a blank slate.

That's the thing Palm Sunday exposes that we don't like to look at too directly. We prefer to think of faith as something clean, a simple, unencumbered transaction between a soul and its Savior. You hear the gospel, you believe, you follow. But the reality is messier than that. Every person who has ever encountered Jesus has encountered Him through something. Through a tradition, a wound, a hope, a fear, a political moment, a cultural assumption. We bring all of it with us. And most of the time, we have no idea we're doing it.

The crowd on Palm Sunday didn't know they were doing it either. That's the first thing we need to understand about them, and about ourselves.

To understand what happened that Sunday in Jerusalem, you have to understand what happened in the four hundred years before it.

The Old Testament ends with a promise. The prophet Malachi speaks, God gives His word, and then silence. For four hundred years, no prophet, no word, and no direct communication was given from God to His people. For four centuries, the Jewish people lived between a promise made and a promise fulfilled, waiting for a Messiah who had not yet come.

But that doesn't mean history stopped. Those four hundred years were full of empire and conquest, of suffering and survival, of moments that would permanently shape the way an entire people understood what deliverance was supposed to look like.

In 336 BC, Alexander the Great became king after his father was assassinated. Shortly after his succession, Alexander began building his empire, sweeping across the known world with an army that moved faster than the news. After Alexander's death, his kingdom fractured. The piece of that kingdom that matters most for our story became the Seleucid Empire, Greek rulers who eventually came to govern the land of Israel. Under their rule, things went from difficult to catastrophic. A Seleucid king named Antiochus marched into the innermost part of the temple in Jerusalem, the holiest place in Jewish life, and defiled it. The wound ran deep, and that pain fueled rebellion.

What followed was one of the most defining moments in Jewish national memory: the Maccabean Revolt. A family of priests, the Maccabees, led an uprising against the Seleucid Empire and won against all odds. And when Judas Maccabeus finally led his people back into Jerusalem in triumph, the crowds responded the way crowds were expected to when their deliverer finally arrived. They lined the streets, cut palm branches, and laid them down in his path as he entered the city. They worshiped a "messianic king" who had saved his people from the oppressive rule of pagan overlords.

Sound familiar?

On that Sunday morning in AD 30, the crowd watching Jesus had this story in their bones. It lived in their cultural memory. The image of a deliverer arriving in triumph, branches on the ground, and the city liberated was the template they carried. They weren't making up how to do it or acting irrationally. This was what they had been taught to expect, and they were pattern-matching. And the pattern fit close enough.

Here is where it gets important. Jesus riding into Jerusalem on a donkey was a direct and deliberate fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy. Five hundred years earlier, the prophet Zechariah wrote: "Rejoice greatly, Daughter Zion! Shout, Daughter Jerusalem! See, your king comes to you, righteous and victorious, lowly and riding on a donkey, on a colt, the foal of a donkey" (Zechariah 9:9). Jesus chose this moment, chose this animal, chose this road. He was announcing Himself as the promised Messiah of the Jewish people. The promise made all those years ago was now being fulfilled before their eyes. The Messiah has come! Here He is!

But here's the problem most people don't recognize. The palm branches they used to celebrate the arrival of the Messiah aren't in the passage in Zechariah. They're never mentioned. There is no Old Testament prophecy, messianic or otherwise, that calls for palm branches to be laid down for the arriving king. What Zechariah gives us is a donkey, a peaceful king, and a promise. Nothing more.

So where did the palm branches come from? They came from the political memory of the people. Let's call it what it is: politicization. The books of First and Second Maccabees are non-canonical historical books. They are not Scripture, but are historical writings that can help us understand more of the culture and background we find in Scripture. In 1 Maccabees 13, we find the story of Simon Maccabeus entering Jerusalem after driving out the Seleucid occupiers. The crowds who welcomed him did so with palm branches and singing. It was this image of liberation from pagan oppressors that burned into Jewish national consciousness. Not a prophetic script. A cultural and political memory of what deliverance looked like the last time it arrived.

Jesus was fulfilling what was written. The crowd was re-enacting what they remembered. Both things were happening simultaneously in the same street on the same morning, and nobody could see the difference.

This is the uncomfortable truth this entire book is built around. Jesus announces Himself on His own terms, but the crowd mistakenly receives Him as something else.

We do this constantly, and we may not even realize it.

Let me give you an example. Let's call her Sarah. She's not a real person, but a composite of a real pattern I've seen across years of pastoral ministry. Sarah came to faith in Christ through a painful season. She arrived at the cross carrying wounds that needed healing, the way most of us do, and Jesus, true to who He is, healed her. She was healed completely and powerfully. The chains that had held her came off, and she was free in a way she had never experienced. Sarah was new, clean, and genuinely transformed.

But then the newness began to settle, and Sarah struggled.

The problem wasn't that her healing wasn't real. It was. The problem was that Sarah had come to know Jesus primarily as her healer, so to her, relating to Him required being sick. As Jesus Himself said, “It is not the healthy who need a doctor, but the sick” (Matthew 9:12). Sarah understood this intuitively, but she drew the wrong conclusion. Instead of moving from patient to disciple, she stayed in the waiting room.

So, Sarah did what many people do when their theology quietly shapes their behavior. Without noticing and without intention, she began to look for and find new wounds. She would surface a fresh hurt, a new struggle, or even a manufactured anxiety and bring it to the Jesus she knew, the one who healed. It was the only way she knew how to be close to Him.

The tragedy of Sarah's story is not that she didn't love Jesus. She did. The tragedy is that she had reduced Him to a single role of healer and then organized her entire spiritual life around perpetually needing that one thing. What had been the doorway into her faith had become the room she never left. The wrong Jesus she carried kept her in exactly the kind of suffering she thought He had freed her from.

What Sarah needed wasn't more healing. She needed a different Jesus.

We all have traditions, experiences, and causes that have brought us to Jesus. There is no wrong way to find Him, and every encounter with the Savior is sacred. But there is a difference between a lens that helps us see and a lens that quietly distorts or replaces what we're looking at. The crowd on Palm Sunday had a lens built from four hundred years of history, oppression, suffering, and longing for deliverance. It was a completely understandable lens. It was totally reasonable and yet still completely wrong. They saw a Maccabean warring king when the Prince of Peace was right in front of them.

When Jesus arrived in Jerusalem that morning, He looked around at the temple courts, took in the noise, the crowds, the chaos of the festival, and then quietly went back to Bethany with His twelve disciples for the night. There was no military action, no political declaration or confrontation with Rome, just a man on a donkey, looking around at a city that had completely misread His arrival.

His Jewish worshipers had to be confused. They had been waiting their whole lives for a moment like this. Finally, after all the years of struggle and oppression at the hands of the Romans, the moment came, and nothing happened. Not yet anyway, and it wouldn't happen as they expected.

That gap between what the crowd expected and what Jesus actually did is not just a first-century problem. It is the hinge on which the entire purpose of this book turns. Because the distance between the Jesus we expect and the Jesus who actually shows up is the space where most of our spiritual confusion lives. It is where disappointment grows and where, if we are not careful, we quietly start editing Jesus, trimming the parts that don't fit and amplifying the parts that do, until the Jesus we are following looks a great deal more like the one we wanted than the One who rode into Jerusalem on His own terms.

The crowd on Palm Sunday was not unique. They were no better or worse than us. They were human, in exactly the way we are human, reading the present through the past, longing for a deliverance that looked like the deliverance they already understood.

The parade they joined that day is not a historical oddity. It is the default human procession that we all, in one way or another, have joined. The question is whether we are willing to stop waving our own palm branches long enough to recognize the Jesus who is right in front of us.

# **CHAPTER TWO**

## **The Jesus We Needed**

Nobody builds their Jesus from scratch.

That's not how it works. We don't sit down one day with a blank page and invent a self-made savior. What we do is far more subtle and very human. We come to Jesus carrying everything we already are. We bring every wound we've absorbed, every message we've received about who God is and who we are to Him. We bring every tradition, every culture, every family system, every moment of joy or devastation that has shaped the way we see the world with us. Without realizing it, we layer it over Jesus like a template, and the Messiah that emerges looks remarkably like the person we wanted Him to be.

The crowd on Palm Sunday did exactly this. They didn't set out to get Jesus wrong. They set out to welcome the Messiah they had been promised. The problem was that four hundred years of history had told them what that Messiah was supposed to look like, and they were no longer capable of seeing past the picture they were already holding. The Jesus they were expecting was shaped long before He rode into Jerusalem. He was shaped by exile, empire, the Maccabean revolt, and centuries of longing for someone to finally make things right again.

Their Jesus made perfect sense given everything they had been through.

That's what makes this so hard to examine in ourselves. The wrong Jesus is almost never the product of rebellion. It is usually the product of our own pain and struggle.

Think for a moment about the most common versions of Jesus that people carry into church on any given Sunday.

There is the Jesus of the wounded. This is the Jesus found by the person who grew up in a home where love was conditional, where approval had to be earned, and where the wrong move meant rejection. That person genuinely and powerfully found Him, and the Jesus they found looked like everything their family wasn't. He was unconditional, accepting, and safe. The Jesus of the wounded is not a false Jesus. He's real, and His grace is real. But over time, without careful attention, this version of Jesus can quietly drift. He becomes a Jesus who never confronts, never corrects, never asks anything difficult, and certainly never challenges us in a way that makes us uncomfortable. This is a Jesus whose primary attribute is that He will never be like the person or people who hurt us. This is a Jesus shaped around a wound.

There is the Jesus of the comfortable. This Jesus was found by the person for whom faith has always been relatively easy. They were raised in a good church, given a reasonable theology, and were never seriously challenged to pay a cost for following Him. The Jesus of the comfortable is good and moral and personally helpful. He improves our life, strengthens our family, and shows up reliably in the places we've already decided to go. He does not tend to ask us to go anywhere uncomfortable. He is, conveniently, already present in the places we were planning to be in anyway. This is a Jesus shaped around comfort.

There is the Jesus of the political. This is perhaps the most visible version in our current climate, and He exists on every side of every divide. This is the Jesus who has been conscripted into a cause, draped in a flag, handed a platform, and made the divine endorsement of a particular vision for how the world should be organized. The Jesus of the political is passionate, morally strong, and somehow always, remarkably, on the side of whatever argument His followers happen to be making. He has strong opinions about policy. He votes the right way. He would be disgusted by the other side.

I have watched this version of Jesus get constructed in real time, from the left and from the right, in congregations and on social media and in the comments section of news articles that have nothing to do with the Gospel. And many times, the people doing the constructing are convinced they are simply following Jesus as He is. They are not even aware they are building Him into anything.

Here is the mechanism behind all of it:

We don't shape Jesus out of dishonesty. We shape Him out of need.

The Jesus of the wounded is shaped by someone who needed to be loved without condition. The Jesus of the comfortable is shaped by someone who needed faith to be manageable. The Jesus of the political is shaped by someone who needed God to be on their side. Every wrong Jesus begins with a real and legitimate human need. That is what makes this idea of Him so convincing. He feels like the answer because He is. The problem is that we are limiting Him in how He answers.

The problem is not where He starts. The problem is where we force Him to be. A Jesus shaped around a wound eventually becomes a Jesus who cannot heal it. A Jesus shaped around comfort eventually becomes a Jesus who cannot challenge you to grow. A Jesus shaped around a political cause eventually becomes a Jesus who cannot save anyone who disagrees with you. The very thing that made the wrong Jesus feel like the answer becomes the thing that limits what He can do.

The crowd on Palm Sunday had a Jesus who had been shaped by centuries of oppression and longing for national restoration. That Jesus was their answer. He was going to fix Rome. He was going to restore David's kingdom. He was going to do what needed to be done.

And then He rode into Jerusalem on a donkey, looked around the temple courts, and went home for dinner.

Not because He didn't care, but because He had come to do something they didn't yet have categories for. Something bigger than Rome. Something that would require Him to go somewhere they absolutely did not want to follow.

I want to be careful here, because it would be easy to read this chapter as an accusation, as if the goal is to make us feel foolish for the Jesus we've been following. That is not my point. It's actually the opposite.

The wrong Jesus almost always begins as an act of faith. The wounded person who reached for a Jesus of unconditional love was reaching for something real and true about who God is. The person who found Jesus in a community with strong political convictions was genuinely trying to live out their faith in the world. The Maccabean crowd who welcomed Jesus as a liberating king was longing for the restoration that God had actually promised them. They weren't wrong to long for it. They were wrong about the shape it would take.

Examining the wrong Jesus is not an act of self-condemnation. It is an act of honesty. It opens the door to a Jesus who is big enough to be who He actually is, rather than who we want Him to be.

The Jesus of the Gospels is not smaller than the ones we build for ourselves. He is larger and stronger. He is more demanding of us, but at the same time, more gracious and more surprising than anything we could construct from our expectations. That is the Jesus this book is trying to help us all find.

But first, we have to be willing to take a hard look inside and examine the Jesus we've already been worshiping.

# **CHAPTER THREE**

## **The Sincerity Trap**

On the morning of April 20, 1999, a man drove his daughter to Columbine High School and watched her walk through the front doors. He had no reason to believe that day would be different from any other. He was a good and attentive father who was loving and present in his child's life. He was also completely wrong about what was waiting inside that building.

His certainty didn't protect her. His love couldn't change the reality of what was unfolding. And no one, hearing that story, would suggest that his sincerity, however genuine, was a safeguard against what happened next.

We understand this instinctively when the stakes are physical. A surgeon who sincerely but wrongly identifies a tumor as benign has still made a fatal error. A pilot who genuinely believes he is flying level when he is not will still crash the plane. We don't console the survivors by saying, "Well, at least he really believed it." Sincerity, in those moments, is beside the point.

So why do we treat it differently when the question is Jesus?

There is a deeply embedded assumption in modern Christianity, and in the broader culture around it, that sincerity is its own kind of currency. That if a person is genuinely seeking, genuinely devoted, genuinely committed to the Jesus they have come to know, then the content of that belief will sort itself out eventually. God honors the heart. God sees the effort. God fills in the gaps.

It sounds compassionate. It is, in fact, a kind of category error.

The crowd that welcomed Jesus into Jerusalem on Palm Sunday was not faking it. We established that in the last chapter. Their shouts were real. Their coats on the road were real. Their expectations were real. They were not cynical performers going through the motions of a religious holiday. They were true believers; genuinely moved, genuinely hopeful, and, ultimately, genuinely wrong.

Five days later, some of the same voices praising Jesus were calling for His execution by crucifixion.

This is the thing about sincerity: it doesn't come with a self-correcting mechanism. Sincerity doesn't ask, "Wait, have I got this right?" Sincerity feels like certainty. That is, in fact, part of what makes it sincere and not mere habit or performance. The more sincerely you believe something, the less likely you are to question it. The feeling of rightness is built into the experience.

Which means that sincerity, by itself, is not a check on error but the fuel for it.

The New Testament does not treat sincerity as a guarantee of anything. Paul, writing to the Galatians, describes his own pre-conversion life in terms that should give every sincere person pause: "For you have heard of my former life in Judaism, how I persecuted the church of God violently and tried to destroy it. And I was advancing in Judaism beyond many of my own age among my people, so extremely zealous was I for the traditions of my fathers" (Galatians 1:13-14).

Paul, the man who would write more books of the New Testament than any other author, whose letters would shape two millennia of Christian theology, was, before his conversion, sincerely, zealously, violently wrong about Jesus. He wasn't a half-hearted skeptic. He was a committed, advancing, tradition-honoring devotee of a religious system that was leading him to persecute, jail, and possibly murder Christians.

Sincerity did not protect him from being on the wrong side of the most important question in history. It took a blinding encounter with the risen Christ on the road to Damascus to break through what his sincerity had fortified.

And notice: Paul doesn't describe his former self as a bad person who didn't care. He describes himself as someone who cared intensely, so intensely that he was willing to imprison and kill for his convictions. That kind of sincerity is not neutral. It is dangerous, precisely because it feels so right.

Here is where this gets uncomfortable, because this chapter is not primarily about Paul before Damascus, or the crowd before Golgotha, or anyone safely in the past. It is about the possibility, the serious and sobering possibility, that a person can be a sincere, devoted, lifelong follower of a Jesus who is, at critical points, not the Jesus of Scripture.

Not a fake Christian. Not a hypocrite. Not someone who has never opened a Bible or never prayed. Someone who did all of those things, and still managed, quietly and over time, to shape the Jesus they follow into something that fits their life more comfortably than the Jesus who actually shows up in the Gospels.

This is not a fringe problem. It is the central crisis of the contemporary church.

We have constructed, in many places, a version of Christian discipleship that prizes emotional sincerity above theological precision. Worship experiences calibrated to produce feelings. Sermon series designed around relevance and felt need. Small groups that major in vulnerability and minor in doctrine. None of these things is wrong in itself, but they create the conditions for sincerity to fill the space that truth is supposed to occupy.

The result is a generation of people who feel deeply about Jesus but have very little idea who He actually is.

Jesus Himself had something to say about this. In the Sermon on the Mount, which we will return to throughout this book, He said something that has always struck me as one of the most unsettling passages in all of Scripture:

"Not everyone who says to me, 'Lord, Lord,' will enter the kingdom of heaven, but the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven. On that day many will say to me, 'Lord, Lord, did we not prophesy in your name, and cast out demons in your name, and do many mighty works in your name?' And then will I declare to them, 'I never knew you; depart from me, you workers of lawlessness.'" (Matthew 7:21-23)

These are not people who weren't devout. They prophesied, they cast out demons, they did mighty works, and they used His name, repeatedly, pointedly, "Lord, Lord." Whatever else we say about them, these were not disengaged religious consumers who showed up only on Christmas and Easter. These were, by any visible measure, sincere followers.

And Jesus says, “I never knew you.”

Not, "I knew you once but lost you." Not, "I knew you, but you drifted." Never. The relationship they were certain they had with Him did not actually exist from His perspective. Their sincerity was not a bond. It was a conviction about a connection that wasn't there.

The question this raises is devastating: Is it possible to be that sincere and that wrong at the same time?

Jesus says yes.

None of this is meant to produce paralysis, or to suggest that assurance is impossible, or to make every sincere believer a nervous wreck questioning their own salvation. That is not the argument. The argument is simpler and more actionable than that.

It is this: sincerity is not enough on its own. It is a necessary feature of genuine faith, but it is not a sufficient one. A person can be sincerely devoted to a distorted Jesus, a Jesus assembled from cultural assumptions, personal preferences, and carefully selected Scripture passages, and that sincerity will not, by itself, course-correct their error.

What does course-correct is contact with the actual Jesus, the One the Gospels present, the One who confounded every expectation, the One who refused to be what everyone expected so He could be what they actually needed. That Jesus is different from the one most of us have constructed. He is also, in the end, the only One with whom we have been called into a relationship.

But before we can meet that Jesus, we have to be willing to confront the possibility that the Jesus we have been sincerely following is, in important ways, a portrait we drew ourselves.

That is the work Part Two is built for.

# **PART TWO: HOW TO RECOGNIZE IT**

# **CHAPTER FOUR**

## **When Jesus Doesn't Do What You Hired Him For**

There is a moment in the Palm Sunday story that almost nobody talks about, and I think that is exactly where we need to start.

Jesus rides into Jerusalem. The crowd is shouting. The branches are down. The energy is electric, four hundred years of longing compressed into a single procession down the Mount of Olives. And then, after all of it, Mark gives us this:

> *"Jesus entered Jerusalem and went into the temple courts. He looked around at everything, but since it was already late, he went out to Bethany with the Twelve." (Mark 11:11)*

One verse. One sentence. The most anticipated arrival in the history of Israel, and Jesus walks in, looks around, and goes home for dinner. That's it.

No declaration. No confrontation. No signal to the crowds that the revolution has begun. No huddle up in a back alley somewhere to bring everyone up to speed on the plan for insurrection. He looked around at everything and simply left. The city that had just erupted in messianic acclaim was still buzzing, still anticipating, and the One they had just praised, cheered, and worshiped went back to Bethany, presumably ate something, and slept.

They had to be beside themselves.

Because what they wanted, what the palm branches and the Hosannas and the Maccabean echoes were all pointing toward, was immediate action. A confrontation with Rome. A political declaration. The moment the long arc of Jewish history had been bending toward and hoping for. They had handed Jesus the perfect stage, and He had walked off to grab a bite and go to sleep.

The morning after is when Jesus clears the temple, and it's worth slowing down here because it's easy to miss what is actually happening.

Jesus returns to Jerusalem. He enters the temple courts and then, in one of the most vivid scenes in all of the Gospels, He drives out the merchants and money changers, overturns their tables, and quotes Isaiah and Jeremiah at the stunned crowd: *"My house will be called a house of prayer for all nations, but you have made it a den of robbers." (Mark 11:17)*

The crowd must have held its breath. Something was finally happening!

But notice what Jesus disrupted. Not the Praetorium (Roman military headquarters), not the palace of Herod, not the garrison of Rome. He disrupted the temple, the sacred center of Jewish life. This is the one institution the crowd believed was already on God's side. He didn't go after the occupiers; He went after the occupied. He didn't attack what the crowd wanted to destroy; He attacked what the crowd wanted to protect.

This is the inversion that should stop us cold. When Jesus finally acts, when the restrained patience of that first evening gives way to something fierce and physical, He moves against religion, not Rome. He disrupts what they thought was already godly. He overturns the tables of the insiders, not the thrones of the empire.

If they had expected a Maccabean king like Judas Maccabeus, who had driven out the pagans and restored the temple, they were sorely mistaken. Jesus drove out the worshipers and called the temple by a different name.

What happens to you when Jesus does something you did not expect?

Not something terrible, just something different from what you anticipated. He heals someone else and not you. He closes a door you had prayed would open. He calls you somewhere inconvenient. He disrupts something you had convinced yourself was already sanctified. He looks around at the thing you built for Him, and His response is not gratitude, but something more complicated.

Most of us have an idea of how Jesus is supposed to show up. It's built on our history with Him, the moments He has come through, the ways He has moved before, and the promises we believe He has made to us specifically. And when He deviates from this idea, there is a gap, and it's this gap that causes confusion. In that moment, the question is never as neutral as it sounds:

> *Why isn't Jesus doing what I expected Him to do?*

That question feels like a reasonable inquiry into the mind of God, but it is often something else. It is a diagnostic signal, a revelation not of God's inconsistency but of our own. Because what we expect Jesus to do reveals what we believe His purpose in our lives is. And what we believe His purpose is reveals the shape of the Jesus we have been following.

The crowd expected a Maccabean king. The temple confrontation was not part of the script. So, they had a choice to make: revise their understanding of Jesus, or revise Jesus to fit their understanding.

Most of the first-century Jerusalem crowd chose the second option. Not immediately. Not consciously. But that is what happened in the gap between Sunday and Friday.

Let's put some feet on this idea and walk it around a bit. Let's talk about Marcus. He's not a real person, but rather a composite of multiple conversations that I have had with real people. Marcus represents a real pattern I have watched play out across years of pastoral ministry.

Marcus came to faith in a season of professional failure. He had built something, a business, a career, an identity, and watched it fall apart. In that collapse, he encountered Jesus in a way that felt unmistakably real. The peace that met him in the wreckage was not something he could explain. The community that surrounded him was not something he could have manufactured. He became, by any reasonable measure, a transformed man.

Then things began to improve. The professional situation stabilized, and a new opportunity emerged. Marcus, understandably, connected the dots: he had trusted Jesus in the valley, and Jesus had led him out. The equation seemed clear. Faith, real, costly faith, produced restoration. That was the pattern. That was how it worked.

So, when the next hard season came, and it did, because life doesn't stop, Marcus brought the same faith. But along with that faith, he also brought an expected outcome. He prayed the prayers. He trusted the process. He gave Jesus the burden of the situation and waited for the restoration. This pattern repeats itself time and time again, until it doesn't. It could happen twice or twenty times, but eventually, the moment arrives when Marcus prays for the relief, restoration, and the reward he expects, and it's nowhere to be found.

It didn't happen the way he expected. The door he was asking God to open stayed shut, and Marcus genuinely, deeply, sincerely, grew quietly furious at Jesus.

When I finally got to sit with him in that frustration, what emerged was something neither of us had named clearly before. Marcus had not been following Jesus; he had been following a pattern he believed Jesus had established, and when Jesus deviated from the pattern, Marcus felt betrayed.

The Jesus he had built was a restorer. He could not be anything else in Marcus's framework because that’s all Marcus knew. That's what Marcus expected because that was where they had met, and that was what the relationship had been established on. Marcus's faith was real. His love for God was genuine. But the Jesus he was trusting was built around a single category, and that category had become both his anchor and his ceiling.

When Jesus didn't restore on schedule, Marcus didn't lose his faith.

He lost his Jesus.

Here is what I have learned to pay attention to in pastoral ministry: the moment of disappointment is not a problem to be managed; it is a question to be asked.

When Jesus doesn't give us what we expect, when He disrupts religion instead of Rome, when He goes home for dinner instead of launching the revolution, when He closes doors we were certain He had promised to open, the most important thing we can do is not to defend our expectations, but to ask what our disappointment reveals, because our disappointment is always about something specific.

We are not disappointed in the abstract. We are disappointed because we expected a particular Jesus to show up or to do a particular thing, and He didn't. And in that gap, if we look at it honestly rather than just filling it with confusion or grievance, we just may find the shape of the Jesus we have been laying down our palm branches for.

This is not a comfortable exercise. It requires a willingness to be wrong about something we probably feel very good about. It requires admitting that the lens through which we have been seeing Jesus might be coloring or distorting what we see. We might be getting Him completely wrong.

But before we beat ourselves up too much, seeing this can also be an act of extraordinary grace, because the disappointment that exposes a wrong Jesus is the very thing that opens the door to the real One.

The crowd on that first Palm Sunday had their template, and Jesus walked right through it and shattered it. He didn't do what they wanted Him to do. And in the gap between what they expected and what they got, the whole drama of Passion Week began to unfold, leading, as we know, to a Friday that looked like the worst possible outcome and a Sunday that rewrote everything.

The disappointment was not the end of the story. It was the beginning of a truer one. The revealing of a better one.

But we have to be willing to let our wrong Jesus be disrupted before we can find the One who actually rode in.

# **CHAPTER FIVE**

## **The Jesus Who Tears Things Down**

> *When the real Jesus destroys what we thought was sacred.*

There is a version of surrender that most of us are willing to make. We will give Jesus the obvious things: the habits we already know are wrong, the attitudes we have privately wanted to be rid of, the patterns of life that embarrass us when we hold them up to the light. We call that repentance. There is nothing small about turning from sin; it's the fruit of our salvation.

But the harder surrender, the one that the crowd on Palm Sunday was not willing to make, is the one Jesus asks for that is not obvious. It is the surrender of what we believe is already sanctified. Not the sin we know about, but the structure we are certain God approves of, or the traditions we believe He ordained. This is the identity we built in His name.

The wrong Jesus never asks for that kind of sacrifice. He endorses, affirms, and multiplies what we bring Him and how we choose to do it. He is completely satisfied with our worship as if to say, "Aww, look, they're trying so hard." As if the attempt is what matters.

The real Jesus expects you to come to Him in His manner of choosing.

This is when Jesus predicts the destruction of the temple.

Mark 13 opens with one of His disciples pointing out the temple buildings, marveling at the scale of them, the craftsmanship, the stones so massive that some of them are still visible in Jerusalem today. And Jesus responds:

> *"Do you see all these great buildings? Not one stone here will be left on another; every one will be thrown down." (Mark 13:2)*

The disciples are stunned. They aren't talking about a pagan monument or a Roman temple of idolatry. They are looking and marveling at the very house of God: The Temple. The second Temple, to be more specific, which was rebuilt and expanded by Herod the Great, and was one of the great architectural achievements of the ancient world. It was also the center of Jewish identity, the place where heaven and earth were understood to meet, and the symbol of everything the covenant had promised and everything the Maccabees had fought and bled to reclaim. This was not something the crowd wanted to change; it was what they wanted Jesus to defend, and He predicted its demolition.

Notice that He doesn't predict Rome's fall, the Herodian palace crumbling, or the destruction of Roman military headquarters. He predicts the temple, the holiest thing in Jewish life, lying in waste, stone by stone strewn across the ground. Jesus did not come to restore what they had built in the name of God, but to replace it with something they cannot even imagine or comprehend. In their minds, this was heresy.

To understand how incomprehensible this idea was, you have to understand what the temple meant to the Jewish people of the first century. The temple was not simply a building or even simply a place of worship; it was the proof that God had kept His promises. It was the proof that Israel was still His people and that their covenant with Him was still intact. Every sacrifice offered there, every pilgrimage made to it three times a year, every stone laid by every craftsman over the decades of its construction, all of it said the same thing: God is still here. God has not forgotten us. God is still God, and we, the Jews, are still His people.

The temple was the physical anchor of their entire theology, and Jesus is saying Rome is going to tear it down and He Himself wept over it (Luke 19:41). He was not dismantling it out of indifference or as a punishment, but allowing it to come down because what it pointed to had already come. God would no longer live in a house of stone but inside each believer. He no longer needed a specific place where His worship would be accepted. What once was the prescription for worship became its barrier.

But the crowd did not see His message as a form of liberation from the confines of the law; they saw a threat. A threat to their understanding of who God was and their relationship to Him; a threat that attempted to dismantle their entire system, and a Jesus who would allow the temple to fall was not the Messiah they had been waiting for or wanted. He was supposed to build and defend, not tear down and destroy.

I've seen this pattern time and time again in pastoral ministry. Someone genuinely and transformatively comes to faith, and in that early season, they build. They build a community, a practice, and even a theology on a set of convictions they believe are God-ordained. They believe He ordained it because they were doing it for Him, never once asking if it was what He wanted.

And then, at some point, whether it's months, years, or decades later, what they built is no longer sufficient. There may be a question the old framework cannot answer, a broken relationship whose weight their theology cannot bear, or a passage of Scripture they have read a hundred times that suddenly reads differently, and the difference is uncomfortable.

Something in them shifts. And the first instinct, almost instantly, is to defend the structure. Because the structure was built for God. It was built in good faith, genuine devotion, and with real prayer. To question it feels like questioning the faith itself. Maybe even questioning God Himself.

Let's walk this out a bit. Say hello to Carol. Again, she's not a real person, but a composite of a real pattern I've seen over the years. Carol had been a faithful churchgoer for thirty-plus years. She raised her children in the faith, served in leadership, and memorized more Scripture than many seminary students I have met. Her Christian life was not showy or spectacular. It was genuine, authentic, and quietly present in everything she did.

Her theology was formed in and by her particular denomination. Who God is, who she is in relation to Him, what He provides, how He works in her life and what she can expect from Him had all been formed by certain teachings and experiences within her Christian experience. These beliefs were the grid through which every question of faith had to pass. They were the lenses through which she evaluated everything. This certainty of who God is and how He works had become a comfort, a silent assurance. It had also done something dangerous that she may not even have realized: it had become a ceiling that limited her perception of what God can and will do.

When one of her children began asking questions regarding how faith and social justice intertwined, her experience did not prepare her to wrestle with what was clearly a stumbling block in the heart of her child. She didn't have the understanding to answer. Her instinct wasn't to see her child's questions as an opportunity to grow and learn together. Her initial response was alarm. The alarm wasn't for her child; it was for her belief system, her theological structure. If everything she ever believed could be put into question in a small discussion with her child, where else could it be questioned? If the knowledge of who God was and how He worked in the world could be shaken, then was it as solid as she believed?

What Carol needed was not a better defense of what she had been taught. She needed what the disciples were given: a Jesus big enough to replace what is sacred in her mind with something greater. But she had to be willing to stand in the rubble first. For Carol, that devastation was too much to bear. That one conversation led her to leave the faith altogether, until, by the grace of God, she returned to the Lord, and allowed Him to construct the truth of who He is.

The temple the disciples admired was a beautiful building. Carol's belief was real faith. Neither Jesus's prediction nor Carol's crisis was an attack on what had been true and good. It was the clearing of space for something new that cannot be built on top of an old foundation. Jesus said, "You cannot pour new wine into old wineskins."

This chapter is not calling all faith traditions evil. It is not an argument that institutions are corrupt, that the structures we build in God's name are inherently suspect, or that the faithful thing is always to tear down the old to build the new. God works through traditions and institutions. He works through the structures human beings build in His name, imperfect as they always are. But He is not bound to them, and He will not be domesticated by them.

The temple Jesus predicted would fall was the temple Solomon had dreamed of, that generations of Jewish people had bled to protect, that the Maccabees had reconsecrated, and the people had rebuilt. It was not a pagan monument. It was sacred, and Jesus knew it was sacred, and He wept for the city that had been built around it. But it was not the dwelling place of God, not ultimately. He was. And when God incarnate arrived in person, the symbol could now be released.

The question this chapter is asking is not: is your tradition bad? The question is: are you holding it with an open hand or a closed fist?

The wrong Jesus will never ask you to open your fist. He'll never ask you to relinquish to Him that which you have built up for Him. The wrong Jesus will just bless what is in your hand and call it complete. The real Jesus might gently, persistently, and sometimes heartbreakingly ask you to set it down, not because it is worthless, but because He has something new to give you that your closed fist cannot receive.

The crowd was holding the temple with a closed fist. They would rather have the symbol than the God the symbol pointed to.

It isn't an ancient problem; it's a Tuesday problem. It is the problem in every season of faith when what we built for God becomes what we are protecting from God. But if we allow Him the authority in our lives to reconstruct what we believe about Him, He will give us the grace to receive it.

Jesus did not let the temple fall without warning. He told His disciples what was coming. He gave them, as He always does, what they needed to make sense of what they would experience, not immediately, not without grief, but eventually. The destruction of the temple in AD 70 was catastrophic for the Jewish people. For the disciples who had followed Jesus, it was, in retrospect, a fulfillment of what He had said would happen. The rubble proved Jesus had known all along. The rubble proved He had been telling them the truth.

When Jesus begins to dismantle something we built for Him, He is rarely silent about it. The loosening we experience, the question that won't quit, the grief that our old framework cannot explain, the Scripture passage that reads differently than it did before, that is not God's absence. That is often His voice, telling us what is coming so that when it comes, we will know that He knew all along.

The disciples did not understand the prediction when Jesus made it. They understood it later, and the later understanding became part of what held them together when everything else was falling apart.

You may be somewhere in that sequence right now, either in the prediction stage, where something is being loosened, and you are not yet sure why, or in the rubble stage, where something has come down, and you are still in the grief of it. Either way, the question Jesus is asking now is the same one He was asking on the Mount of Olives, looking at the temple His disciples were admiring:

> *Are you willing to follow Me when I dismantle what you thought was sacred?*
>
> *What in your life would you resist surrendering to Jesus, and why do you consider it protected?*

# **CHAPTER SIX**

## **Going Somewhere You Didn't Sign Up For**

> *What following actually costs, and what it reveals.*

There is a particular kind of spiritual disappointment that nobody warns you about. It isn't the disappointment of unanswered prayer, or suffering, or the uneasy stillness of silence in a dark season. The church has at least developed a vocabulary for those things. The disappointment I'm talking about is quieter and, in some ways, more disorienting. It is the disappointment of following Jesus faithfully and ending up somewhere you never intended to go.

That is exactly what was happening in the week between Palm Sunday and Good Friday. Jesus was moving deliberately toward a destination, and it was not the one His followers had in mind. They signed up for a coronation. They were being led toward a cross, and the distance between those two destinations was straining everything.

The anointing at Bethany makes the direction undeniable. A woman comes with an alabaster jar of expensive perfume, breaks it open, and pours it over Jesus's head. The disciples react with indignation. What a waste. Jesus reframes the entire scene with a sentence that must have landed hard: “She has done a beautiful thing,” He says. “She has anointed my body for burial.”

Burial. Not a coronation, not a campaign launch, not a brainstorming session about new Kingdom agendas, just burial. These men had followed Jesus for three years, had watched Him feed thousands, silence storms, and raise the dead, and now all of it pointed not toward a throne room but toward a tomb. Whatever map they were carrying in their heads, this was definitely not the "X marks the spot" destination they had in mind.

Most of us have a map. We may not have drawn it consciously, but it is there, an internal picture of where following Jesus is supposed to lead. Obedience produces blessing. Faithfulness results in stability. Surrender is rewarded, eventually, with a life that makes sense. The map is not entirely wrong; there is enough Scripture to sketch it and enough testimony to fill it in. The problem is when the map quietly becomes a contract. That is where the trouble starts.

A contract-faith sounds like this: I will give myself to Jesus, and in return, things will go well. I will follow Him, and in return, the path will be one I recognize. The disciples were running exactly this version of faith. Three years of miracles had convinced them the trajectory was upward, and the destination was going to be triumph. Then the destination turned out to be a garden arrest, a late-night trial, and a borrowed tomb. They scattered, every single one of them, not because their faith was fake, but because their map was wrong.

I have been on both sides of this, and I want to be honest about it. There was a season in ministry when I knew what God was asking, and I did it faithfully and consistently, at real personal cost. I gave up something comfortable for something harder because I believed Jesus was asking me to. Then the hard thing got harder, the cost got higher, and I found myself in a place I had never agreed to when I originally said yes. My conversation with God in that season was neither joyful, thankful, nor theological. It was, essentially: I did not sign up for this.

I don't think God was offended by the conversation. He let the silence that followed my heartfelt expression teach me something I had needed to learn for a long time. The question that came back to me, slowly, the way real things tend to, was: when exactly did you negotiate the terms? I had been operating as if following Jesus was a transaction I could review and approve, as if discipleship came with a clause allowing me to object when the destination turned out to be inconvenient. The silence was telling me, gently and without cruelty, that I had confused following with consulting. I was willing to walk with Jesus anywhere He chose to go, provided He chose somewhere I already wanted to go. That is not following anything. That is just walking in the same direction for a while.

The anointing at Bethany is doing something specific in the story. Jesus is not surprised by where the road is going. He has known since before the triumphal entry. He orchestrated that entry, chose the animal, chose the moment, fulfilled Zechariah 9:9. All of it with full knowledge of what was waiting at the end of the week. He is not stumbling toward the cross. He is walking toward it with open eyes, telling His disciples plainly and repeatedly that the Son of Man will be betrayed, condemned, mocked, flogged, and killed, and on the third day will rise (Mark 10:33-34). He says it with the calm of someone who has already made peace with the itinerary God has planned. He says it to people who hear every word and cannot absorb any of it, because the destination does not match their map or their expectations.

The woman with the alabaster jar heard something they missed. She may not have been able to articulate the theology of what she was doing, but she responded to where Jesus was actually going rather than where everyone else assumed He was headed. She anointed Him for burial while the disciples were still debating seating arrangements in the kingdom. She was oriented towards the real destination; they were lost because Jesus walked off their map.

In my family, we have a phrase we come back to in those seasons when God asks for more than we budgeted. We call it obedience over comfort. It is simple, and yet it is hard at the same time. Obedience over comfort doesn't mean suffering is inherently righteous or that the harder path is automatically a holier one. It means something more specific: when following Jesus and maintaining personal comfort come into direct conflict, discipleship chooses following, every time. Not because comfort is evil, but because the moment you subjugate your obedience to your convenience or comfort, you have quietly replaced Jesus with your own preferences and given the arrangement a spiritual name.

The Jesus of the Gospels does not call people to manage risk and incremental improvement. He calls fishermen away from their boats and a tax collector away from his table. He tells a rich young man to liquidate everything. He calls twelve ordinary people to follow Him into Jerusalem in a week when He already knows exactly how the week will end. If your version of following has become entirely comfortable, the honest question worth asking is whether you are following Jesus or following your own idea of where He would go if He were more like you.

The wrong Jesus will never take you somewhere that costs you anything you weren't already prepared to spend. He is endlessly accommodating, perpetually affirming, and conveniently headed in the direction you were already planning to travel. The real Jesus walks toward a cross and asks if you are coming.

There is a prayer I have heard prayed sincerely, with full hearts, which has always given me pause. It sounds something like this: “Lord, I am willing to go wherever you lead, just please make it somewhere I understand, with comfortable conditions, and a use for the skills I already have.” I say this with some affection because I have prayed a version of this prayer myself on more than one occasion. The instinct behind it is not faithlessness; it is actually very human. We want to give ourselves to something larger, but we want to enjoy it. We want to be led, but we would like advance notice of the route.

The disciples on the road to Jerusalem wanted exactly that. They were willing to follow Jesus into the city, but they were not prepared for the week that was actually coming. When the arrest happened, they had nothing left to hold because the map they'd been following was a projection of their own hopes, not a portrait of their actual Savior. The map always fails when Jesus goes somewhere it does not show.

Following Jesus to a destination you did not choose is not a sign that something has gone wrong. It can often mean something is going exactly right. The disciples who scattered in the garden were not uniquely faithless; they were following a self-shaped Jesus who would never have gone anywhere like that. When the real Jesus went there anyway, they had nothing to hold onto. The disciples were lost because Jesus walked off their map.

You may be in an unplanned and possibly uncomfortable destination right now: a conversation you've been avoiding, a surrender you keep deferring, a step you've been waiting to understand before you take it, or something similar. The disciples threw their cloaks over the colt before they understood what they were doing. They served before they had answers and followed without a map that made sense. It was the disciples, not the crowd, who were still in the room when the story turned.

The crowd celebrated Jesus all the way to the city gate. The disciples followed Him into the week. The difference between the two is not how loudly you shout Hosanna. It is whether you keep walking when the road goes somewhere you didn't agree to in advance. The grace is that He never asks you to go somewhere He hasn't already been.

> *Where is Jesus asking you to go right now that you've been resisting? And what would one step of obedience look like today?*

# **CHAPTER SEVEN**

## **Choosing Barabbas**

> *What we do when Jesus fails to deliver*

The crowd did not walk away from Jesus. This is the thing we need to understand before we go any further. On Good Friday, the people who had lined the streets five days earlier with palm branches and Hosannas did not arrive at Pilate's courtyard in a state of cynicism or apostasy. They were not done with God. They were not done with deliverance. They were not done with saviors, or at least they were done with this particular one. They were ready for a replacement.

This is what makes the Barabbas choice so theologically significant. He is not the absence of redemption. He is a substitute savior. The crowd shouting for his release was not abandoning the hope they had carried into Jerusalem on Sunday; they were redirecting it. Jesus had not delivered what they came for, so they found someone who would. Someone who stood for exactly what Jesus had refused to become: an insurrectionist, a fighter, a man with blood on his hands and Rome in his sights. They did not stop worshiping. They didn't stop shouting. They changed who they directed their hope to, and they did it in a single morning.

Mark tells us that Barabbas was in prison with the insurrectionists who had committed murder in the uprising. That detail is not incidental. Insurrection is organized, purposeful violence against the government. Barabbas was not a common criminal. He was a revolutionary who had done what the crowd that had laid palm branches had spent four hundred years dreaming someone would do: he had taken up arms against the pagan oppressors. He brought violence to Rome. Clearly, he had also failed, because he was in jail. This means whatever uprising he had been part of had not succeeded. He was sitting in a cell. None of that mattered to the crowd. What mattered was what he represented. He was a revolutionary. Even though he had not succeeded in freeing the Jewish people from their Roman occupation, he had at least tried.

Jesus had not tried at all. He had ridden into the city on a colt, looked around the temple, and gone home for dinner. He had cleared the temple courts. Not of Romans, but of merchants and money changers, His own people's religious apparatus. He had predicted the destruction of the temple His people loved so much. He had let Himself be arrested in a garden without a fight, told Peter to put away his sword, and gone quietly into a trial He could have ended with a single word. Jesus was clearly not the Messiah they had been waiting for, so they chose one who at least had the right enemies and the right instincts, even if he had the wrong outcome. A failed revolutionary was still a revolutionary. A dead reformer was still just a teacher. They knew which one they wanted.

When the real Jesus fails to deliver what our wrong Jesus promised, we don't stop worshiping. We change who we are worshiping, and we call the substitute by the same name.

This is the most dangerous part of this book, partly because it is the most common one. It does not look like apostasy. It does not feel like abandonment. It feels like faithfulness. It looks like faith resting on results, like refusing to accept a Jesus who would not fight, like staying loyal to what you originally believed the Messiah was supposed to do. The crowd on Good Friday would not have described themselves as betrayers. They would have said they were holding out for the real thing. That is exactly what makes it so hard to see in ourselves.

Let me describe what this looks like in practice because it rarely announces itself well.

It looks like the person who came to faith during a season of desperate need. During a financial collapse, a failing marriage, or maybe a health crisis, they met Jesus, who showed up in the gap and made a way when there was no way. That experience was real. The rescue was real. But slowly and without intending to, the person organized their entire relationship with Jesus around the expectation of rescue. Jesus became their emergency fund, their crisis manager, and their insurance policy. When the next crisis doesn't resolve itself the way the last one did, and Jesus doesn't make a way this time like He did last time, something shifts. The prayer life cools. They start looking for something that will give them what they came for, and they don't call it leaving Jesus. They call it being realistic, growing up. What they have actually done is swap the Jesus of the Gospels for a Barabbas who will deliver the specific thing they decided they needed.

It looks like the pastor who entered ministry with a clear and confirmed calling, who spent years building something for the Kingdom, and who watched the thing he built fall short of what he believed God had promised. The church stayed small, the influence didn't expand, and he began drifting, slowly and almost imperceptibly, toward a version of ministry that was less about what Jesus was doing and more about what would finally get him where he felt God had said he would go. He hadn't abandoned the faith. He had found a Barabbas who wore ministry clothing.

It looks like the activist who came to faith because Jesus is genuinely the champion of justice, but who has slowly allowed their chosen cause to absorb Christ until the cause is primary, and Jesus is just the mascot. The activism is doing the work only worship can do, and the disappointment of a world that doesn't change fast enough has produced a Jesus indistinguishable from a political program. They're often still in church, but they are following an activist Barabbas and calling him Lord.

The pattern is always the same: a real encounter with a real Jesus, a specific need or hope the encounter generates, a moment when Jesus does not deliver that specific thing, and then, quietly, gradually, with full sincerity, a substitute who will.

What I find most unsettling about the Barabbas moment is the role of the religious leaders. Mark tells us the chief priests stirred up the crowd. They did not manufacture the disappointment, which was already there, accumulating since Sunday. What they did was amplify and direct it. They took a crowd that was already disillusioned and handed them a target for that disillusionment, filling the gap between what the people had hoped for and what Jesus had delivered with the name Barabbas. Ideologues and demagogues have always done this. The ideologue can only see the world through the lens of a chosen ideology. The demagogue can only lead by manipulating people's emotions and fears. Together, they do not create disappointment. They find it, name it, and weaponize it. They find people whom the wrong Jesus has already let down, and they offer a substitute who will do what the real Jesus did not.

We are surrounded by people who will stir us, who will take our legitimate disappointments, our unanswered prayers, our unhealed wounds, our unrealized hopes, and offer us a Barabbas who will finally get it done. It may be a political movement that promises the Kingdom of God through the mechanisms of power. It may be a prosperity theology that promises the riches that Jesus never did. It may be a therapeutic spirituality that only knows how to comfort and never how to challenge. It may be a social cause so consuming that it has displaced the Christ it claimed to serve. All of them begin with a real grievance, all of them offer a real form of action, but none of them are the real Jesus.

Mark's account puts something uncomfortable directly in front of us. Do you follow Jesus because you want someone who will fight your enemies, win your battles, or vindicate you against the people who have wronged you? On Good Friday, you would have chosen Barabbas. Do you follow Jesus because He validates the politics you already believe in, whether left or right, progressive or conservative? On Good Friday, you would have chosen Barabbas. Do you follow Jesus because He accomplishes whatever you have decided is more important than what He actually came to do? On Good Friday, you would have chosen Barabbas. The moment any cause becomes the primary reason you follow Him, the moment Jesus becomes the instrument, and the cause becomes the master, you have stopped following Jesus and started following the version your cause requires. And that version will eventually disappoint you because the real Jesus refuses to be reduced to our self-centered ideas and causes.

The crowd on Good Friday was not uniquely faithless. They were people who had invested everything in a real hope, rooted in real promises, shaped by real history, and who had watched that hope walk quietly past every opportunity to become what they wanted it to be. Their grief was not manufactured. They did what hurt people do: they looked for something that would finally give them what they wanted. They found Barabbas and called it deliverance.

The merciful irony of the story is that the cross they demanded turned out to be the very thing that saved them. The Jesus they rejected was doing, in that rejection, the only thing that could accomplish what they actually needed most. They were wrong about what they needed, but God was not wrong about what He was doing. That is not a license to keep choosing Barabbas, but it is worth knowing that our misdirected longings do not defeat the God who works all things together for good. He is patient with them in a way that should make us honest about them.

Name your Barabbas, not as an act of shame, but as an act of clarity. What have you turned to, or what are you being tempted to turn to, because Jesus has not delivered what you wanted? What substitute has been offered to you that promises to do the thing the real Jesus hasn't done yet? You cannot release what you haven't identified, and you cannot follow Jesus until you have stopped following the thing you put in His place.

> *What is the Barabbas in your life? What is the substitute you have reached for when Jesus did not deliver what you wanted?*

# **PART THREE: WHAT IT COSTS US**

# **CHAPTER EIGHT**

## **Loud Voices**

> *Who handed you your Jesus, and do you know their name?*

Nobody builds their picture of Jesus in isolation. We like to think of faith as a private transaction, something that happened between us and God in a moment of genuine, unmediated encounter. There is something real in that; the moment of genuine faith is real, and the encounter is real. The problem is that the picture of Jesus we brought into that moment, the categories we used to receive Him, the frame through which we understood who He was and what He had come to do, that picture was handed to us. By someone. Usually by several someones, over a period of time, in ways we may not even be aware of.

The crowd in Pilate's courtyard on Good Friday did not arrive at their verdict independently. Mark is careful to tell us this. The chief priests stirred up the crowd. The word used here translated "stirred" is not a gentle one. It means to incite, to agitate, or to cause an uproar. It is the language of deliberate provocation by a small group whipping up a larger group toward a specific outcome. The crowd did not spontaneously decide to ask for Barabbas. They were driven to it. They likely did not notice they were being manipulated because the anger that resulted felt entirely like their own. That is how stirring works. The best version of it leaves no fingerprints. You end up holding a conviction that feels like it came from inside you, from your own discernment, your own reading of the situation, and you can't even feel the hands that shaped it. The question is whether you know whose hands may have left invisible fingerprints in your heart and mind.

The chief priests in Mark 15 were not acting out of theological conviction. Pilate knew this and Mark tells us so explicitly. They were playing a political chess game with religiously painted pieces, protecting power and influence that Jesus was threatening simply by existing. They went to work on the crowd and found the disappointment that was already there, the accumulated disillusionment of a week that had not delivered what the crowd believed Palm Sunday promised. They then directed it toward a target and a demand: release Barabbas, crucify this one. The crowd was not wrong to feel disappointed. The disappointment was real. What they could not see was that the people fanning the flame of their disappointment had reasons of their own for steering it in that direction. The religious leaders were not helping the crowd find the truth; they were using the crowd to protect themselves. Hurting, confused, and looking for somewhere to put their grief, the crowd followed the loudest voices, voices they should have been able to trust, without ever asking what those voices were getting out of it.

I say this not to condemn them, but because I have done exactly the same thing, and I suspect you have too. Hurt makes us vulnerable. Pain makes us susceptible. Disappointment makes us hungry for a narrative that turns confusion into clarity or pain into purpose. The people who offer those narratives in our moments of hunger, the preachers, the platforms, the personalities, the political leaders, do not always have our formation or even our best interests in mind. Sometimes they do. But many times, they have something else they are protecting.

We live in an environment of unprecedented noise. The volume itself is not new; every generation has had its loud voices and its gatekeepers of received opinion. What is new is the scale, the speed, and the intimacy of it. A preacher in a previous century could shape a congregation of hundreds or a readership of thousands. A voice today can reach millions before breakfast, delivered directly into the ear through a device that most people sleep beside. The mechanisms of stirring have never been more efficient, and the mechanisms of stillness, of going directly to the source rather than receiving it filtered through someone else's interpretation, have never faced more competition.

The result is that most people who consider themselves followers of Jesus are, in practice, followers of their favorite interpreter of Jesus. Their picture of who He is, what He cares about, what He demands and what He permits; all of it has been significantly constructed by voices they have chosen to trust, often without examining the terms of that trust very carefully. This is not entirely avoidable; we all need teachers, and the New Testament assumes we will have them. The problem is not having teachers. The danger is having only teachers and losing the ability to go to the source and read it yourself, in the quiet, without someone else's conclusions already ringing in your head. When the interpreter becomes the authority, when you know what your favorite preacher thinks about a passage better than you know what the passage actually says, you have made yourself precisely as vulnerable as the crowd in Pilate's courtyard: full of feeling, ready to be directed, and unable to tell the difference between what you have been told to believe and what you actually know.

This is not a wholesale dismissal of teachers and preachers. God does not need a filter to speak to you. That is not a critique of preaching, it is a statement about the sufficiency of Scripture and the direct accessibility of God. The Word has been written. The Spirit has been given. Every follower of Jesus has been granted access to the primary source in a way that would have astonished believers in any previous century. We have the complete canon, translated into our own language, available in more formats than any generation in the history of the church. The resources for going directly to Jesus have never been more available, and most people are not using them. Why? I think the answer is dangerously simple. The secondary sources of social media, podcasts, eBooks, and emails are so much more convenient and immediately satisfying. A podcast gives you someone else's conclusions without the work of arriving at your own. A sermon clip gives you a moment of insight without requiring you to sit with the text long enough to be surprised by it. A social media feed gives you an endless supply of other people's applications without requiring you to encounter the thing itself. This is not neutral. Every layer of interpretation between you and the Word Itself is a layer of someone else's potentially wrong Jesus standing between you and the real One.

The concept of stirring implies a pre-existing state of rest that was disturbed. You stir something that has been sitting still. This means the antidote to being stirred is not simply better information, it is stillness. It is the kind of quiet, regular, unhurried engagement with Scripture that is not driven by a question someone else put in your head, not shaped by a controversy that arrived in your feed this morning, and not filtered through the conclusions of the voice you most trust. It is the kind of thing that happens when you are molded gently by the text, over time, allowing it to form the questions as well as provide the answers. The stirrers, whoever they are and wherever they come from, cannot fully deconstruct or redirect what you believe in because you have already gone to the source. They can add to what you know, but they cannot build your Jesus from scratch because you have been reading the Word for yourself.

If the primary way you encounter the teaching of Jesus is through sermons, including mine, hear this as a direct pastoral warning: it is not enough. Sermons are one voice, delivered weekly and are directly shaped by one person's reading of the text, filtered through one person's history, emphases, and yes, their blind spots. A good sermon opens a door; it is not meant to be the room. If Sunday morning is the sum total of your engagement with Scripture, you are depending entirely on someone else's picture of Jesus rather than developing your own, and no preacher is a reliable enough person to bear that weight.

If the primary way you encounter Jesus is through social media, through clips, quotes, comment threads, and the theological opinions of people you follow, then you are in the courtyard. You are in exactly the environment where stirring is most effective, where the loudest voices shape the most opinion, where the distance between a manufactured outrage and a sincere conviction has been engineered to be invisible. The platforms are not neutral; they are designed to produce engagement, and engagement is most reliably produced by strong emotion. Strong emotion about Jesus, whether it be anger, fear, tribal loyalty, righteous indignation, or something else, is one of the most effective engagement generators available. You are not exempt from that mechanism because you are a Christian. You may, in fact, be more susceptible to it because the stakes feel higher when the subject matter is Jesus.

The correction is not complicated, though it costs time and attention. Read the Gospels. Not commentaries about the Gospels, not podcasts explaining the Gospels, not social media clips of people responding to other people's interpretations of the Gospels. Read Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John. Read them slowly enough to be surprised. Read them without a predetermined conclusion about what they will say. Read them until you find things that do not match what you expected, because if you have been reading long enough and have not been surprised, you have not been reading. You have been confirming. Go to Jesus directly. He is not hiding, and He did not write in a code that requires specialists to decode. He spoke to fishermen, tax collectors, Samaritan women, and Roman soldiers in language they could all follow. He did it then and still does today. The question is whether we will sit still long enough to hear Him, or whether the noise of the courtyard has become so familiar that silence feels like absence.

When Jesus stood in Pilate's courtyard, Pilate asked the crowd a direct question: what shall I do with the one you call the King of the Jews? They answered without deliberation, without hesitation, with an unanimity that tells you the answer had already been prepared for them: crucify Him. They did not ask why. They did not go back to what they had witnessed on Sunday or what they knew of the Scriptures. They repeated what they had been given, and when Pilate pushed back, they repeated it louder. Why? Because that is what stirred people do. Escalation feels like conviction, but the two are not the same thing.

Pilate's question is still being asked of us in every format our contemporary moment delivers. What will you do with Jesus? The only answer that is truly yours is the one you arrive at by going to Him directly, not the one handed to you by the loudest voice in the room of your life. Know the voice you are hearing. Know what they are getting out of handing you their picture of Jesus. Then put the phone down, open the pages of Scripture, and let Jesus speak for Himself. He has things to say that no one else has said quite right.

> *Where is your picture of Jesus coming from? And when did you last read the Gospels for yourself, without a filter?*

# **CHAPTER NINE**

## **The Quieter Sin**

> *The person who sees clearly and does nothing.*

Pilate is the most uncomfortable figure in the Passion narrative, not because he is the most villainous, because he isn't. The chief priests are more calculating, Judas more intimate in his betrayal, the crowd more volatile. Pilate is something subtler and, in a certain light, far more recognizable. He is the man who knows exactly what is happening, says so out loud, and then participates in the folly anyway. He is not deceived. He is not inflamed. He is not operating out of self-interest the way the chief priests and the crowd are. He is simply unwilling to bear the cost of acting on what he already knows.

That is a particular kind of failure, and it is one that does not get enough attention in discussions of Good Friday, because it is far easier to locate ourselves in the crowd, confused, stirred, caught up in a moment we did not fully understand, than to locate ourselves in Pilate's intentional actions. The crowd could at least claim they were misled. Pilate cannot. He understood the situation with precision, chose the path of least resistance anyway, and that choice killed an innocent man. This chapter is about that kind of failure: not ignorance, not deception, not passionate misdirection, but the failure of the person who sees clearly, knows better, and goes along anyway because the cost of not going along is more than they are willing to pay.

Mark gives us Pilate's inner knowledge in a single verse, and it is one of the most damning lines in the whole narrative: Pilate knew it was out of self-interest that the chief priests had handed Jesus over to him (Mark 15:10). Read that slowly. Pilate knew. He was not operating in the dark, not taken in by the religious leaders' framing of Jesus as a threat to public order. He could see behind the accusation to the motivation that produced it, and he knew the motivation was transparently political. The chief priests had a problem. Jesus was destabilizing their influence, drawing crowds they could not control, teaching things that undermined their authority. They turned to the machinery of Roman justice to solve it. Pilate could see all of this and said so. Then, he turned to the crowd and asked what he should do with Jesus, which is itself a verdict. The Roman governor, the representative of Caesar, the one person in that courtyard with the actual legal power to end the proceeding, turned to the crowd for a recommendation. See this. Pilate's turning to the crowd is not because he lacked authority. He had all the authority on that day in that place. What Pilate lacked was the will to use it. He had examined Jesus, found no basis for a charge, recognized the chief priests' motives as corrupt, and had everything he needed to do the right thing. He spent the rest of the scene looking for a way to hand the decision to someone else.

The more precise word for what Pilate was doing is not cowardice, though cowardice is part of it. The word is outsourcing. He retained the title, the office, and the legal power. The Roman centurions still answered to him. What he surrendered was the willingness to exercise the authority he had. He handed the effective decision to the crowd, who were being directed by the chief priests, who were in turn protecting their own interests. When the crowd shouted for crucifixion, Pilate could tell himself, and perhaps even believed, that he had simply done what the people wanted. He could claim he had been a responsive leader and the outcome was not his doing.

It was entirely his doing. Full stop.

Pilate's outsourcing and pandering to the crowd did not transfer the responsibility of the final decision; it just allowed him to deceive himself into feeling better about the miscarriage of justice he was allowing to happen.

This pattern shows up everywhere once you learn to see it. It shows up in institutions that know a problem exists and route it through enough committees that no single person ever has to own the decision to address it. It shows up in relationships where someone watches a destructive dynamic continue because naming it would be harder than tolerating it. It shows up in churches where leadership knows something is wrong; a doctrine drifting, a person in authority causing harm, or a pattern of behavior damaging people, and the response is to commission a report, wait for more information, and give it time. The machinery of deceptive deference is very good at producing the feeling of process without producing decision. It also shows up in our spiritual lives. It's in the person who knows what Jesus is asking and has been finding sophisticated reasons not to do it. Not in rebellion exactly, because rebellion would at least be honest, but in something harder to name. In a permanent state of almost-obedience, perpetually one more piece of information away from acting on what they already know.

Matthew's account adds a detail that stops you cold. During the trial, Pilate's wife sends him a message urging him to have nothing to do with this innocent man. She had dreamed about Jesus the night before and was troubled. Pilate receives the warning seriously enough to hear it. He is not entirely closed. He knows, and now he has been told again. Then he calls for a basin of water and washes his hands in front of the crowd, declaring his innocence of the matter. The handwashing is one of the most psychologically precise details in the entire Passion narrative, performed not privately as a personal ritual of conscience but publicly, as a gesture meant to communicate something to the audience:

I am not responsible for this.

This blood is on your hands, not mine.

He is not trying to convince himself. That decision has already been made. He is managing his legacy in real time, curating how the moment will be remembered, and distancing himself from an outcome he is in the act of producing. It did not work. Two thousand years later, his name is forever recorded in the Apostles' and Nicene Creeds: suffered under Pontius Pilate. History assigned him the responsibility he tried to wash off.

I have sat with people over the years who were living in a version of Pilate's courtyard. They were not bad or uncommitted people. They were, in many cases, people who cared deeply about doing the right thing and who could articulate, with some precision, exactly what was happening and why it was wrong. Knowing was not the problem. What was missing was the willingness to bear the cost of acting on it. One man had been watching an ethical compromise in his workplace grow for years into something he could no longer look at directly. He knew what it was and what he should do, and he had very good reasons for why the timing was never right. The cost came in his ethical leadership to his family. It gnawed away at the hope things would resolve on their own or that someone else was better placed to raise the concern. Every reason was real. Yet none of them were the actual reason he never acted. The real reason he never wanted to admit was that doing the right thing would cost him something he was not yet willing to pay. He was not a dishonest man, but he had learned to be fluent in the language of just-not-yet. Sustained long enough, just-not-yet becomes never and good character becomes comfortably corrupted with deception.

Another woman had known for a long time that her silence in a particular relationship was enabling something harmful to someone she loved. She had the words, the relationship, the standing to say something that might matter, and she had very sincere reasons for why the moment was never quite right. The person was too fragile. The relationship was too important to risk. She needed to pray more first. She was waiting for the right opening. All of it was true. Yet none of it was the real reason. The reason, in both cases, was the same reason driving Pilate: the right action was clear, the cost of the right action was real, and the gap between knowing and doing was being filled with process instead of action. Time ticked away with reasons and delays and the performance of consideration in a way that felt like faithfulness but was functioning as avoidance.

The honest answer to how often we tolerate, submit to, or participate in things we know are against the Kingdom, whether out of fear of what those around us will say, or how the comment section will read, is more vividly real than we would like to admit. We live in a moment that makes Pilate's particular failure quite easy to fall into, because the cost of dissent has rarely been more visible or more immediately delivered than it is today. The crowd in Pilate's courtyard was loud and momentarily present. The crowds we face today are global and perceptibly permanent. The fear of the crowd has always been a human vulnerability, and the voice of the crowd has never had as much reach as it does today. So, we develop the same habits Pilate did. We find the language of process, call for more study, seek more nuance, bide more time, and perform more consideration of doing the right thing without arriving at any end decision. We wash our hands in public and hope the gesture is sufficient. We outsource the authority we have been given, the power to name what we see, to act on what we know, and to say the true thing when it is costly. We silently sell it to the loudest voice in the room so they can face the blowback instead of us.

That is the other deceptive thing about Pilate's failure: it does not feel like a decision. It feels like a series of small accommodations, each one reasonable, none of them irreversible, right up until the moment they are. The choice to crucify Jesus was not made in one dramatic moment of evil but arrived at through small yieldings: to the crowd's pressure, to the chief priests' framing, to the path of least resistance, to the desire to get through the morning without a crisis. Each step was manageable. Reasonable even. The destination, however, was catastrophic.

The needed correction in our cultural moment today is not heroism. The temptation when confronted with this kind of failure is to reach for dramatic resolution or to perform some brash, poorly planned, public, and costly act of defiance. Sometimes it is that. More often though, what is needed is smaller and more private. It is the one-on-one conversation you have been avoiding, finally had. The thing you know is wrong, named in the room, to the person who needs to hear it, with your own voice. The decision you have been processing for six months, made and action taken. Pilate had the power; the text is explicit. With one wave of his hand, the Roman centurions would have defended Jesus. The problem was not the absence of authority. It was the unwillingness to use what he had. Most of us have more relational or positional authority in our specific situations than we act as though we do. We have the standing to say something, the relationship to make the call, or the clarity to know what is right. What we lack is the willingness to bear the cost of using it.

That willingness is not produced by more information or more process. It is produced by the settled conviction that the One whose opinion ultimately matters has already weighed in. The Jesus who stood in Pilate's courtyard without defending Himself, the Son of God who could have called twelve legions of angels and chose not to, who bore the cost of Pilate's cowardice without returning it, is calling us to follow Him into exactly that kind of costly faithfulness. He does not ask us to be fearless. He asks us to act in the presence of fear. This is the difference between a faith that waits for perfect conditions and one that moves with what it has, in the moment it has it, knowing that the One who goes before us has already been to the hardest place the road can reach.

Pilate had a chance to be part of that story. Now, so do we.

> *Where have you outsourced your spiritual authority to the crowd? And what would it look like to wave your hand?*

# **PART FOUR: FINDING THE REAL JESUS**

# **CHAPTER TEN**

## **Jesus Has Never Been Ambiguous About Who He Is**

We have spent the better part of this book inside a single week, walking the distance from palm branches to crucifixion nails, watching the wrong Jesus of the crowd collide with the actual Jesus of the Gospels, and examining the mechanisms of disappointment and substitution and complicity that produced Good Friday from Palm Sunday in the span of five days. It is time now to go back to the beginning of that week, not because we haven't visited it. We started there in Chapter One, and it has been the reference point for everything since. We are going back now with different eyes. Eyes that have traveled the road from Sunday to Friday and hopefully can see differently now.

Here is what the road reveals when you have walked it: the problem was never Jesus. The confusion was never because of Him. The distance between what the crowd expected and what Jesus was actually saying did not exist because He was ambiguous or hidden or deliberately obscure. From the first moment of that Sunday morning, the deliberate choice of the animal, the road, the fulfillment of a five-hundred-year-old prophecy, Jesus was entirely, precisely, and openly declaring Himself. The mistake was ours. It was then and it always has been. Seeing that clearly is the first movement of reorientation.

Let's go back to the first verse of Mark 11. "As they approached Jerusalem and came to Bethphage and Bethany at the Mount of Olives, Jesus sent two of His disciples." The first thing Mark wants us to see is that Jesus initiates. He is not being carried along by the momentum of the crowd. He is not responding to the energy of the festival or improvising as the moment unfolded. He directs His disciples ahead with specific instructions about a specific animal in a specific village. He prepares them for the specific objection they will encounter and gives them the specific response that will resolve it. He arranged this. The entry into Jerusalem is not a mistaken or misread proclamation that happened to Jesus. This is an intentional procession that Jesus designed.

This matters more than it might initially seem. A passive Jesus, one who was swept into the triumphal entry by the crowd's enthusiasm or who accepted the palm branches and Hosannas because the moment called for it, would be a very different figure from the one Mark is describing. A passive Jesus could be shaped for people or forces outside of Himself. He would be available to become whatever the crowd needed or wanted Him to be, because He would have no fixed agenda of His own. That is not Mark's Jesus. Mark's Jesus has already decided what this morning means, what it announces, and what it fulfills. He chose the humble colt of peace not the radiant stallion of war (that comes later). He chose the prophet's animal, not the general's. He was making a theological statement in the plainest possible language, in broad daylight, in front of thousands of people. The crowd misread His announcement. That was their failure, not His. He was not speaking in code. They simply read what they wanted into the situation and then accepted something Jesus never communicated.

There is a question that comes up when you look carefully at this scene: how did Jesus know the colt would be there? The honest answer is that the text does not say. There are three possibilities, and the text supports all of them, so you're free to choose. The first possible answer is God's omniscience. Jesus is the divine Son of God and divine knowledge is not bounded by ordinary limits. He could simply know, the way God knows, without the mechanism being available for explanation. The second option is prophetic knowledge. Even in His full humanity, Jesus was filled with the Holy Spirit, and the Spirit who inspired the prophets of the Old Testament to speak things they could not have known by ordinary means was present in Jesus too. The third possibility is the one that tends to get overlooked because it is the most human: Jesus made the arrangements. He had been to Jerusalem many times and knew people there. He could have spoken to the family who owned the colt on a previous visit. He could have said simply, I will need to borrow your animal on a certain day; hold it for me. No divinity required, no prophetic vision required, just a person who knew what He was going to do and planned ahead accordingly.

All three possibilities together say something important: Jesus does not need to be stripped of His humanity to be trusted. We do not need to reach immediately for the supernatural explanation to find Him reliable. He is fully God and fully man. The fully man part of Him is not a reduction or a concession, it is part of the incarnate revelation. A Jesus who could make a reservation, who could plan a detail, who could think ahead and act on that thinking, is not a lesser Jesus. He is a more accessible one. He is a Jesus who inhabits the same kind of world we inhabit, makes the same kind of decisions we make, and is trustworthy in both the divine and the ordinary.

There is a detail at the end of Mark 11 that I have always found quietly remarkable. After the triumphal entry, after the crowds and the palm branches and the Hosannas, after riding into the city at the center of a procession visible and audible from a considerable distance, Jesus went to the temple, looked around at everything, and because it was already late, went back out to Bethany with the twelve. He went back the way He came, and somewhere on that road back to Bethany, He returned the colt to the family who had lent it. The text does not make a production of this. The detail is embedded in the geography since Bethany is where Jesus started and where that village sat along the same road. The implication is clear: Jesus did not keep what He borrowed. He made arrangements to use the animal, and He honored those arrangements by returning it.

That seems like a small thing. It is the kind of small thing that tells you who someone is. A person who keeps His word about the borrowed colt is the same person who keeps His word about everything else. A person who honors the dignity of the family who lent Him their animal, who does not simply take what He needs and leave the accounting to others, is a person whose integrity operates at the level of the ordinary, not just the spectacular. This is Jesus who is inviting us to reorient our lives around Him and His Kingdom. Not the abstract theological figure, not the projector figure for your needs and hopes, not the wrong Jesus constructed from your wounds, your politics, or your cultural inheritance. The One who planned an entry into Jerusalem with the care of someone who intends to do it right. The One who chose a colt of peace when a legion of war-horses and angels was available. The One who borrowed an animal, used it for the prophetic purpose He planned, and then brought it home.

Jesus has never been the problem. Every wrong Jesus we have examined in this book, those shaped by politics, wounds, comfort, or cause were all constructed in the space between what Jesus actually is and what we want Him to be. But that space exists solely on our side of the encounter, not His. Jesus rode into Jerusalem on a donkey, fulfilling a prophecy written five centuries before His birth, in broad daylight, in front of a city full of people who had access to that prophecy. He was not hiding. He was not ambiguous. He was not sending mixed signals. He was being, as completely and publicly as possible, exactly who He is, the promised Messianic Savior King. The mistake has always been in reception, not the transmission. We bring lenses He never asked us to bring and see through them so naturally that we mistake our tinted or warped vision for clear sight. We misread Jesus through our political movements, our wounds, our cultural inheritances, and the interpretations of the voices we trust most. The sad part is we can do this for years without ever realizing we are misreading Him because reading feels just like seeing.

The work this chapter begins is not acquiring new information about Jesus. It is learning to put the lenses down. Or, more precisely, learning to see that you are wearing them in the first place. This is harder than it sounds, and the difficulty is usually underestimated. The lenses we bring to Jesus are not arbitrary; they are the product of real experiences, real pain, real communities, real encounters with something genuine. The person who met Jesus as healer and organized their whole spiritual life around that one role did not invent the experience. The healing was real. The person whose political convictions have slowly migrated into their theology started from a real conviction that justice matters and the world should be different. These lenses aren't nothing. They are the raw material of genuine faith, bent slightly out of shape by the pressure of what we needed or wanted them to become. Setting the lenses down is not as simple as deciding to. It involves honest grief. It asks us to acknowledge that the Jesus we have been following may not be entirely the Jesus of Scripture. It requires the willingness to be surprised by the real Jesus, which means tolerating the disorientation of encountering someone who does not match the picture we've been carrying.

The self-shaped Jesus, however comfortable He may be for us, is always a smaller thing than the real King. There is more of Him than the versions we have been following.

Read the Gospel of Mark this week. The whole thing, it is the shortest of the four and it moves fast. Read it as if Jesus is trying to tell you exactly who He is, not as if you are searching for confirmation of what you already believe. Read it as if someone you thought you knew has handed you a letter that says: let me tell you something about myself that you may have missed. Then notice the moments when He surprises you, when He does something you did not expect, refuses a role you would have given Him, accepts a person you would have turned away, or challenges someone you would have spared. Those moments of surprise are the places where the real Jesus is showing through the shaped one. They are not problems to solve; they are the thing itself. They are the real Jesus, arriving on His own terms, into the space where you were expecting someone slightly different.

He has been doing this the whole time. The triumphal entry was full of it. The week that followed was nothing but it. He kept being precisely Himself through every human attempt to make Him into something else, right up to and through the cross, which was itself the most complete and costly refusal to be what everyone around Him needed Him to be. He did this all in the service of becoming what we all actually need. That Jesus, the real Jesus, is still arriving, still on the road from Bethphage, still choosing the colt, still heading for the city on His own terms rather than ours every time we read the pages of Scripture. The question is not whether He is there to be found. The question is whether we are willing to lay down our palm branches long enough to see who is actually riding toward us.

> *Read the Gospel of Mark this week as if Jesus is trying to tell you exactly who He is. What surprises you?*

# **CHAPTER ELEVEN**

## **The Disciples as Servants Who Simply Kept Walking**

There is a quiet contrast buried in the Palm Sunday scene that is easy to miss because the crowd is so much louder. The crowd is the story by every external measure. The palm branches, the Hosannas, the cloaks spread on the road, the volume, the energy, the visible participation, they all clamor for and monopolize our attention. If you had been standing on that street, the crowd is what you would have surely seen. The crowd is what the Roman soldiers noted. The crowd is what made the Pharisees nervous enough to tell Jesus to silence His disciples, to which Jesus replied that if the disciples were quiet, the stones themselves would cry out.

Mark, in his characteristically economical way, has been tracking a smaller group within and around that crowd from the very beginning of the scene. Two disciples go ahead at Jesus's instruction to retrieve the colt. The rest gather around as He mounts it. They are the ones who throw their cloaks across the animal's back providing a saddle Jesus never asked for, serving a comfort they simply noticed was needed. They walk with Him into the city, go with Him to the temple, and are the ones He takes back to Bethany when the evening comes. The crowd celebrated Jesus for a morning. The disciples followed Him into the week. That difference, the subtle difference between celebrating and following, between the enthusiasm of Palm Sunday and the faithfulness of what came after is what this chapter is all about. The disciples are not exalted here as examples of spiritual excellence. We know they fail spectacularly within a few days. They are held up as something more accessible and more useful than heroes. I present them to you as something much simpler and significantly more powerful. We can look at the disciples as faithful followers who kept walking in the right direction even when they did not fully understand where they were going.

Go back to the moment of the cloaks because it deserves more attention than it usually receives. When they brought the colt to Jesus, the disciples threw their cloaks over it and He sat on it. Nobody told them to. The instructions Jesus gave were specific: go to the village, untie the colt, answer the question you will be asked with the answer I am giving you, and bring the animal back. Cloaks are not mentioned. The saddle is not part of the directive. The disciples simply observed that Jesus was about to ride an animal that had never been ridden, with nothing between Him and the animal's back, and they did something about it. They gave to Jesus what they had, their own outer garments, the thing a person in that culture wore to protect against the cold and rain and dust of the road. They gave it to Him not to show His divinity or announce His upcoming anti-Roman military campaign, but to make the ride more comfortable for the One they were following. It is a small act, not a miracle, not a theological statement, just people noticing a practical need in the person they love and meeting it with what they have. The palm branches came from the crowd's emotional enthusiasm. The cloaks came from the disciples' humble attention. Enthusiasm is loud, easily available, and usually does not cost much. Attention is quiet. It is specific. It requires you to actually see the person you are following and think about what's next.

The second mark of their following is harder than simply being attentive. The disciples followed without full understanding. They had been with Jesus for three years by the time they walked into Jerusalem that Sunday. Three years of watching Him work, listening to Him teach, traveling with Him through Galilee and Judea and the regions beyond. They had seen enough to trust Him, but they had not seen enough to comprehend Him. Those are very different things. Mark keeps the confusion visible. The disciples misunderstand parables that seem clear. They argue about greatness on the road to Jerusalem, right after Jesus has predicted His death for the second time. They sleep in the garden when He asks them to stay awake and pray. They are not models of spiritual insight. What they are, most of the time, is present and willing. When Jesus says go do something, they go, even when they do not fully see why.

Go to the village and untie a colt that belongs to someone else. They go. Follow me into Jerusalem on the day I ride in as the fulfillment of Zechariah 9:9, in a city full of people who are about to misread the whole thing and spend the next five days accumulating the disappointment that will produce Good Friday. They follow. Come with me to the temple, watch me look around at everything and then walk back out without doing anything visible, then walk two miles back to Bethany for the night. They follow. They do not understand the full arc of what is happening. They won't understand it until after the resurrection, when the Spirit opens the Scriptures to them and the whole shape of the week becomes clear. But in the midst of that lack of full knowledge, they do not wait for understanding before they follow and obey. They have learned, somewhere in three years of watching Jesus not be wrong, that understanding is not the prerequisite for following. It is often the reward that comes after.

The contrast worth naming is this: if we have to understand everything before we obey, we are not worshiping Jesus. We are worshiping our own understanding, which is a subtle and dangerous form of idolatry because it feels so responsible. It feels like wisdom, like the kind of careful, considered faith a thoughtful person ought to have. We are not going to leap blindly into something we cannot verify. We are going to be prudent, ask good questions, wait for good answers before we move. In many areas of life that is exactly the right approach, but it becomes a spiritual problem when applied to the specific, personal invitations of Jesus. These are the ones that arrive not as doctrinal propositions to be evaluated but as direct calls to a particular act of obedience. Go to the village and untie the colt is not an idea or proposition. It's a call. It's a directive. The disciples' response was not to deliberate and debate whether they had enough information to proceed responsibly. It was to go.

There is a version of this so-called sophisticated faith that has, in practice, become a permanent holding pattern, always asking questions, always processing, always almost ready to commit, always one more conversation or retreat or sermon series away from the actual step. The questions are real, and the processing is genuine, but the function of the whole apparatus is the same as Pilate's handwashing. It allows a person to feel engaged with the decision while perpetually deferring the moment of actually making it. The disciples were not sophisticated in that way. They were, if anything, almost comically underprepared for most of what Jesus asked of them. They didn't have the theological framework to fully understand what the triumphal entry meant or the emotional capacity to handle what the week was about to bring. What they had was the willingness to keep walking toward the person they were following, even when the road went somewhere they had not expected. That willingness turned out to be enough. Not because it was heroic, given what happened in the garden, but because Jesus, working with the willingness that was offered, produced something out of it that the disciples themselves could not have generated.

Most of us are not being asked to untie a literal colt. The calls that come to us tend to arrive as quiet convictions rather than audible instructions. It's the sense that a particular conversation needs to happen, or a particular relationship needs more of you, or a particular act of generosity or honesty or sacrifice is being asked for in a specific moment. They tend to be modest in scale and costly in the particular currency they require: time, comfort, reputation, control, the right to be understood before you move. The question they put to us is the same question the disciples faced on the road to Bethphage: will you go before you fully understand? Will you throw your cloak across the animal's back before anyone asks you to? Will you walk into Jerusalem with someone who is heading somewhere you cannot yet see, because you have enough experience of who He is to trust where He is going?

I have been asked that question in my own life more times than I can count, and I must admit, I have not always answered it the way the disciples did. There have been moments when I waited for more clarity than was going to be given. I held onto my cloak and let the sophistication of my processing become a way of avoiding the simplicity of the step. What I have learned through those moments is that the clarity I was waiting for rarely arrived in advance. It almost always arrived on the other side of obedience. It materialized in the experience of having walked toward Jesus with what I had and finding that it was enough for the next step, even when it was not enough for the whole journey.

The Palm Sunday crowd is not entirely gone from the story after Good Friday. Some of them became a part of the three thousand on Pentecost. Some were in the upper room. Some saw the risen Jesus in Galilee. The crowd was not irredeemable. The crowd as a crowd was not capable of the kind of following the week required. Crowds celebrate; individuals follow. The movement from celebrating to following is almost always a movement from the general enthusiasm of a shared moment to the specific, personal, costly decisions of an ordinary day. The disciples made that movement, imperfectly and with significant failure along the way, but they made it. The tradition of the church that has carried the gospel across twenty centuries is not the tradition of the crowd. It is the tradition of the ones who kept walking. The ones who went back to the upper room after the crucifixion, who stayed until the Spirit came, who then went out into a world that had just executed their teacher and told that world with their lives as the evidence that He was alive.

They did not understand everything when they started. They were still arguing about greatness when He was predicting His death, still falling asleep in the garden, still scattering at the arrest. The risen Jesus met them where they were, recommissioned them with what they had, and sent them out anyway. That is the model this chapter offers: not heroism, not perfect comprehension, not the kind of faith that has worked everything out before it steps. The model is the cloak on the colt, a practical, unasked-for act of love offered to the person you are following. Offered by someone who does not yet know how the week ends but knows enough about who Jesus is to keep moving toward Him.

You don't need to understand the whole road. You just need to take the next step, and the next step in almost every case is simpler than we have made it. It is the conversation we have been avoiding, started. The act of generosity we have been deliberating, done. The surrender we have been processing, made. The cloak we have been holding onto, offered. The disciples went to get the colt without knowing everything it meant. That is the whole invitation.

> *What is Jesus asking you to do right now that you don't fully understand? And what would one step of obedience look like today?*

# **CHAPTER TWELVE**

## **You Cannot Surrender What You Have Not Named**

Here is something that may feel like it undercuts everything we've been through so far: you cannot think your way out of a wrong Jesus. You can follow every argument in this book, nod at every diagnosis, recognize yourself in every portrait of the crowd or the disciples or Pilate, and still walk out with the same wrong Jesus you walked in with. Understanding is necessary, but it is not sufficient on its own. The wrong Jesus does not live primarily in our theology. He lives in our habits, our prayers, and our instinctive responses to disappointment. He rests in the categories we reach for without thinking when life gets hard and we need to know where God stands. Removing Him from those places requires something more than correct information. It requires practice, honesty, and repeated deliberate returns to the real Jesus. Slowly, over time, this will replace the self-shaped image of the wrong Jesus with the actual One.

Not as a formula or a guaranteed technique, but as a set of honest practices. These are things you can actually do, in the ordinary day to day, that move you in the direction of the real Jesus rather than away from Him. Before we get to the practices, we have to do the naming, because you cannot lay down what you have not identified. Identifying the wrong Jesus you've been following is harder than it sounds, because He feels like Jesus to you. He has always felt like Jesus. That is the whole problem, and it does not stop being the problem just because you have read a book about it.

## **The Wrong-Jesus Inventory**

What follows is a set of honest questions. They are not designed to produce shame or guilt. They are designed to produce clarity. Read them slowly, and stay with the ones that produce resistance, because resistance is usually information. The questions that make you want to move on quickly are often the ones most worth staying with.

**1. When Jesus disappoints you, what has He failed to do?**

The answer reveals what you actually believed He was for. Not necessarily what you would say in a small group, but what you feel at the moment the disappointment arrives. If the feeling is betrayal, you were operating under a false contract. If the feeling is confusion, you may have been carrying a set of directions that do not match the map. Name the expectation underneath the disappointment as specifically as you can.

**2. What in your life would you not want Jesus to touch?**

Every person has a zone of protected territory they fiercely guard. Maybe it's a relationship, an ambition, a political commitment, a wound, an identity, or a way of seeing the world. The fact that you would resist surrendering to the authority of Jesus is the big red flag. The wrong Jesus conveniently has no interest in that territory. The real Jesus tends to walk directly toward it. Name yours.

**3. Who gave you your picture of Jesus and what were they getting out of the version they handed you?**

This is not an invitation to cynicism about your tradition or your teachers. Most of the people who shaped your picture of Jesus were doing so in good faith, but every human teacher is well, human. This means every teacher has influences that come from their own history, wounds, and interests. Whose voice has been loudest in constructing your image of Jesus? What did they care most about? Did they consistently leave anything out?

**4. Does your Jesus ever surprise you?**

A wrong Jesus does not surprise you, because He is a projection of what you already believe. If you cannot remember the last time you encountered something in the Gospels that unsettled you, contradicted your expectations, or made you genuinely uncertain about a position you had been confident in, the Jesus you have been reading may not be the One who is actually on the page.

**5. What do you need or want Jesus to be that He has never claimed to be?**

This is the most personal question on the list and the most important. It is the question underneath all the others. The wrong Jesus almost always begins here, with a legitimate need that reaches toward God but lands on a self-shaped construction rather than the real thing. Political vindication. Emotional comfort that requires no change. Tribal endorsement. Personal ambition dressed in Kingdom language. Name what you have needed or wanted Him to be as honestly as you can, then ask whether the Jesus of the Gospels has ever made that promise.

## **The Practice of Gospel Re-Reading**

The previous two chapters have already recommended reading the Gospel of Mark, and I am recommending it here a third time. Not because I have run out of things to say, but because the repetition is the point. The wrong Jesus is resilient. He is rebuilt quickly from the materials of daily life, without our active participation. Going to the source is not a one-time corrective; it is an ongoing discipline.

Choose one of the four Gospels, Matthew, Mark, Luke, or John, and read it straight through in as few sittings as possible. Do not read it with a question you have brought from outside the text. Don't start it with a sermon series in mind or with a theological debate you are trying to resolve playing in your head. Read it with a single orienting question held lightly in the background: who is this person? As you read, mark every moment when Jesus does something you did not expect, not something theologically surprising in an abstract sense, but something that surprises you personally, given the specific Jesus you have been carrying. He forgives someone you would not have forgiven. He challenges someone you would have spared. He ignores a question you considered important. He refuses a role that seemed obviously His to play.

Do not resolve those moments too quickly, and do not reach immediately for a commentary that explains the surprise away. Stay with the surprise itself, because the surprise is the gap between the wrong Jesus and the real One in your heart and mind. The surprise is a gift because it is the place where the real Jesus is most visible, precisely because He is doing something your constructed version would never do. Over time, not in one reading but in many, the accumulation of those surprises begins to do something. The wrong Jesus becomes less convincing because he keeps failing to match what the text actually shows. The real Jesus becomes more specific, more three-dimensional, more genuinely other than you. That is the beginning of an encounter rather than a confirmation.

## **The Posture of the Disciples**

The present, attentive, willing to move before they fully understood posture the disciples held on Palm Sunday is available to you right now, in the circumstances you are actually in. It does not require a first-century road or a borrowed colt. It requires a settled decision, renewed daily, that you are going to keep walking toward the real Jesus rather than toward the version of Him that requires the least of you.

For most people, this looks like three things. The first is honesty in prayer. Not the performance of prayer or the presentation of a coherent spiritual self to a God who already knows what is underneath it, but an actual conversation with God, including the parts that are uncomfortable. What are you disappointed about? What are you afraid of? What are you holding back? The disciples were not polished in their conversations with Jesus. They said wrong things, asked self-interested questions, and fell asleep when they should have been awake. Jesus kept talking to them anyway, kept correcting and recommissioning them, because they were in the conversation rather than managing their distance from it.

The second is Scripture engagement oriented toward formation rather than information. There is a way of reading the Bible that is essentially extractive, mining it for content that confirms what you already believe or provides ammunition for positions you have already decided to hold. There is another way that is slower, quieter, and much more submissive. This happens when you come to the text willing to be questioned by it, willing to have your categories reorganized by it, and willing to find out that Jesus has been saying something different than what you thought you heard. The first produces a more sophisticated wrong Jesus. The second produces the conditions for encounter with the real One.

The third is community with people who do not share your blind spots. The wrong Jesus is almost always a product of a particular community's emphases and omissions. You can find this in the things your community or faith tradition talks about frequently and the things it quietly leaves out. No tradition has the whole picture. Exposure to followers of Jesus from genuinely different backgrounds, traditions, and experiences of God is one of the most effective checks on the tendency to mistake the part of Jesus your community has seen for the whole of who He is.

I think about that mud-brick church in Tanzania, the worship I could not understand a word of, but still sounded unmistakably like the Church of God worshiping the Son of God. My African brothers and sisters had seen and experienced Jesus in a way that I had not seen. They had encountered dimensions of His faithfulness that my tradition, with all its strengths, had not surfaced for me. Standing in that room was not an ecclesiological challenge, it was a theological expansion. The Jesus I encountered there was not a different Jesus. He was the same Jesus, more fully seen than my own context had shown me. That is what genuine community across differences does. It does not compromise your picture of Jesus. It enlarges it.

## **A Prayer of Surrender**

What follows is a way of naming the Jesus you built, specifically and honestly, and releasing him in the direction of the real One. It is not a formula. It is a structure for saying out loud what has often only been felt, and for directing that honesty toward the One who can do something with it. Say this, or something like it, in your own words:

Lord Jesus, I have been following a version of You that I helped construct. I built him from things that are real: from my wounds, from my needs, from the communities that handed me their picture of You, from the hopes I carried into faith and the disappointments that came after. The Jesus I built felt like You because I was reaching for You when I built him. But I know, if I am honest, that I have also been reaching for what I needed him to be. Those two things are not always the same.

I name what I have needed You to be that You have not promised: [name it here, specifically, in your own words].

I name the zone of my life I have kept You out of: [name it].

I name the voice that has most shaped my picture of You, and I release that picture to Your correction: [name it].

I ask You to show me who You actually are, not the version that costs me nothing, not the version that fits neatly inside my politics or my wounds or my ambitions, but the One who rode into Jerusalem on a borrowed colt, returned it when He was done, looked around the temple with clear eyes, walked toward a cross He could have refused, and rose from a tomb that could not hold Him.

I am willing to be surprised by You. I am willing to follow without understanding. Help me put down the Jesus I made. I want the One who is actually there.

Thank You Lord Jesus for Your patience and leadership in my life.

Amen.

> *Name the Jesus you built. Then open the Gospel of Mark and read until you find someone who doesn't match.*

# **Conclusion**

## **Not a Resolution, an Orientation**

There is one more thing to say about the crowd on Palm Sunday. They were not wrong about everything. The things they shouted were true. "Hosanna, Lord, save us" was the right word for the right person. "Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord" was a direct quote from Psalm 118, and it was accurate. The one they were welcoming was, in fact, the fulfillment of the Davidic promise. He was, in fact, the Son of God entering His city. He was, in fact, the Savior the world had been waiting for across four hundred years of silence. Their theology, found in the words they chose, was spot on.

It was the palm branches that were wrong. The imported expectations underneath the Hosannas were wrong. The Maccabean template they were reading Jesus through was wrong. The instinct, however, the bone-deep longing that had driven them into the street that morning, which had produced the volume and the energy and the genuine, unmistakable passion of the moment...

All of that instinct was aimed at exactly the right person.

They were reaching for the real thing.

They were just receiving it through a lens that bent it into something it was not. I find a great deal of mercy and comfort in this. Not because it is a license to warp my view of Jesus into something that makes me feel better, but as a reminder that the finding of a wrong Jesus almost always begins as a reaching out toward the real One. The construction is not the opposite of faith. It is faith filtered through our humanity before it has been purified by encounter with the actual living risen Savior. The crowd was not faithless. They were human. So are we.

The question this book is built around does not stay answered. You can read these twelve chapters, pray the Prayer of Surrender, read the Gospel of Mark twice, and arrive at a genuine, honest, Spirit-assisted clarity about the wrong Jesus you have been carrying, and then quietly, over the next six months, build a new one. From new materials, shaped by new pressures, assembled so gradually that you may not even notice until the disappointment arrives again and you find yourself standing in a courtyard you recognize all too well.

This is not a counsel of despair. It is a call to be realistic about what it means to be a human being following a God who exceeds us in every direction. The shaping and molding is not a sin to be conquered once and left behind. It is a tendency to be watched for, a weakness to be checked repeatedly, and always met with the same honest return to the source every time it is discovered.

"Am I following Jesus or am I following what I want Him to be?" is not the kind of question you answer in a single sitting and move on from. It is the kind of question that becomes a permanent feature of the spiritual landscape. It surfaces in different forms at different seasons, asking to be engaged with rather than resolved.

The disciples who walked through that week with Jesus did not stop needing correction after the resurrection. Paul had to confront Peter publicly, years later, about a wrong Jesus who conveniently aligned with the social pressures of the moment. The churches that received the New Testament letters were full of people who had met the real Jesus and were already constructing substitutes. The history of the church across twenty centuries is, in no small part, the history of people who loved God genuinely, got Jesus wrong repeatedly, were corrected, drifted, and were corrected again.

We are in that company. We are not the first people to mistake the Messiah, and we will certainly not be the last. What we have is the same thing that every generation before us has also had. We have the authoritative and sufficient resources that help us keep finding our way back. We have the Scriptures and the Spirit. We have a community of those asking the same honest questions from their own angles and traditions. We have the real Jesus who has never, not once, hidden who He is. The same Jesus who rode into Jerusalem, in broad daylight, on a borrowed colt, fulfilling a five-hundred-year-old promise, returning the animal when He was done, is not difficult to find. The only challenge is receiving Him on His own terms rather than ours.

The crowd that got Jesus wrong on Palm Sunday was shouting for His crucifixion by Good Friday. By Pentecost, some of them, perhaps many of them, were also among the three thousand who responded to Peter's preaching in the street and were added to the church in a single morning. The mistake was not the end of the story.

The Jesus who refused to be what the crowd needed Him to be on Sunday is the same Jesus who, by Friday, was doing the only thing that could actually save them. He died the death their wrong expectations never could have produced. He purchased the forgiveness their self-shaped Messiah never could have provided. He was, in the rejection, crucifixion, and resurrection, being precisely and completely Himself, delivering on His own terms the very thing they needed most. Not liberation from Rome, but liberation from everything that makes us capable of standing in a courtyard and shouting for the death of the One who came to save us. He knew what they needed better than they did. He always does.

That is the final word of this book, offered not as a comfortable resolution but as a foundation stable enough to build a following life on: the Jesus who exceeds your wrong version of Him is not a threat to your faith. He is the answer to it. The real Jesus, stranger than you expected, more demanding than you planned for, more tender than you deserved, more committed to your actual flourishing than any constructed version ever could be, is better than the one you built. In every direction. Without exception.

You will drift toward the wrong version again. So will I. The question will surface in a new season, wearing a new face, asking to be engaged honestly. Engage with it every time. That engagement is not a sign of weak faith. It is what faithful following looks like from the inside. It is the ongoing work of a person who has decided that the real thing, however uncomfortable, is worth more than a comfortable substitute.

The crowd laid down their cloaks on the road that Sunday morning. So did the disciples. Both groups gave what they had. Only one group kept walking when the road went somewhere they had not planned. Keep walking. Ask the question. Lay down what you built. Look up, because He is still coming toward you, on His own terms, and He is exactly who He says He is.

> *Am I following Jesus, or am I following what I want Him to be?*
